## Frameworks Found

### 1. The Bigfoot Test

**Core Mechanism:** A method for evaluating whether a claim is more likely to be a hallucination (positive pattern-matching on noise) or a real observation. Adams argues that most political and media "hallucinations" are positive hallucinations -- people adding something to the environment that is not there, rather than failing to see something that is.

**Key Quote:**
> "Imagine two hunters go off into the woods. When they come back, one hunter says, 'We just had a conversation with Bigfoot.'... Typically, a hallucination is a 'positive' hallucination -- you're adding something to the environment that isn't there." (Ep 194)

**Application:** Adams applies this to any claim where people "see" evidence of racism, collusion, or corruption that is ambiguous. The test: is the observer adding a pattern to ambiguous data, or is the pattern objectively present? If the "evidence" requires mind-reading or interpretation of intent, it is more likely a Bigfoot hallucination.

**Extended Application -- Mob Boss Frame (Ep 195):**
> "Notice what they did. They said there's no evidence of any crime, and then they described the pattern as being consistent with a mob boss... Apply the Bigfoot Test: if someone is hiding something, does the pattern of their behavior look different from somebody who isn't hiding something? There's no way to tell." (Ep 195)

**Episodes:** 194, 195, 196

### 2. The 48-Hour Rule

**Core Mechanism:** When a public figure says something provocative that generates outrage, you must wait 48 hours for clarification. The rule: accept the clarification, not the original statement. This applies to all parties.

**Key Quote:**
> "If you say something provocative that gets the critics chattering, you've got 48 hours to clarify. And the rule is that you accept the clarification, not the original statement." (Ep 185)

**Application in Practice -- Amy Siskind / Zina Bash (Ep 208):**
> "I tweeted out that the 48-hour rule was in effect. People said, 'For whom? For Amy Siskind who blamed her for being white power, or was the 48-hour rule for Zina Bash who did that allegedly?' I said both. The 48-hour rule is optional, so anybody can clarify anything they want. The rule is that if they clarify, you accept the clarification." (Ep 208)

Adams notes that Siskind took down her tweet (retraction) and Bash's husband clarified. Both sides used the 48 hours. Adams calls the system "working."

**Episodes:** 185, 200, 208

### 3. Two Movies on One Screen

**Core Mechanism:** The same set of facts produces two completely incompatible narratives depending on which "movie" you are watching. Neither side can see the other's movie. The framework is diagnostic, not prescriptive -- Adams uses it to explain why political discourse is broken, not to say one movie is correct.

**Key Quote:**
> "There are two versions of reality, and each side believes that the other side's reality is fake news." (Ep 199)

> "If there's one way to know if somebody is wrong, it's when they say, 'Everything on my side is right and everything on your side is an illusion.' You could be pretty sure that's wrong because the illusions are certainly diversified." (Ep 199)

**Application -- Cohen/Manafort (Ep 192):**
> "The people on the left looked at Cohen and Manafort and said, 'This is the beginning of the end. This is worse than Watergate.' The people on the right said, 'None of this has anything to do with the President or Russian collusion.'" (Ep 192)

**Application -- Woodward Book and NYT Mole (Ep 215):**
Adams notes the unintended consequence: the mole's account paints the President as someone whose staff pushes back on him successfully, which contradicts the "dictator" narrative. Two incompatible movies are being promoted simultaneously by the same opposition.

**Episodes:** 192, 196, 199, 215

### 4. Directional Accuracy vs. Factual Accuracy

**Core Mechanism:** For leadership and persuasion purposes, the *direction* of a claim matters far more than its factual precision. A leader who is directionally correct but factually imprecise will outperform one who is factually precise but directionally irrelevant.

**Key Quote (whiteboard lesson):**
> "The truth has two components: the accuracy... and the direction... Accuracy never matters [for leadership]. The direction matters every time." (Ep 196)

**Application -- North Korea:**
> "The entire game with North Korea is directional. If things are going in the right direction, at any rate, we're winning." (Ep 215)

Adams argues this is why Trump's imprecise statements work: they are directionally correct even when fact-checkers score them as false.

**Episodes:** 196, 215

### 5. Predictive Pattern Analysis (Which Pattern is More Predictive?)

**Core Mechanism:** When evaluating whether an investigation or line of attack will succeed, ask: is the *number* of avenues of investigation evidence that something will be found, or is the *duration without finding anything* more predictive?

**Key Quote:**
> "Which of those patterns is more predictive? Does the fact that there are so many avenues tell you that something will be discovered? Or do you look at it and say, 'After three years of all that looking, we've got basically nothing.'" (Ep 197)

Adams argues the duration-without-results pattern is far more predictive than the number-of-angles pattern. This is his core framework for evaluating the Mueller investigation.

**Episodes:** 197, 215

### 6. The Anti-Trump Loop

**Core Mechanism:** A self-reinforcing cycle where Trump does something, critics call it wrong/crazy/racist, the thing works, critics move to the next thing, and the cycle repeats. Identified via Mollie Hemingway's observation. Adams argues the loop is invisible to those inside it.

**Key Quote:**
> "What I've noticed is Mollie Hemingway's observation, which is the 'anti-Trump loop.' It goes like this: Trump does something, the critics say it's the end of the world, it works out okay, and then Trump does the next thing. The critics never notice that they were wrong about the last thing." (Ep 188)

**Application -- Compressed Cycles (Ep 215):**
> "The half-life of a criticism is shorter now. The criticism was 'he's a crazy clown,' and that lasted for months. Then it turned into 'he's a racist,' and that lasted months... I wonder if we're in a desperation mode for the critics and they're going back to their 'Best Of.'" (Ep 215)

Adams tracks the acceleration of the loop as a predictive indicator of opposition desperation.

**Episodes:** 188, 215

### 7. Mind-Reading Detection

**Core Mechanism:** Any analysis that claims to know another person's inner mental state -- their motives, fears, obsessions -- is not evidence. Adams calls this "mind-reading" and considers it the single most common analytical error in political commentary.

**Key Quote:**
> "If you call me a sycophant, you're making assumptions about what's in my mind... You do not have the ability to understand the inner thoughts of people who are objectively and unambiguously not like you." (Ep 205)

**Application -- Woodward Book (Ep 208):**
> "Look for the mind-reading. Look for how many times the book starts with a fact and then it devolves into mind-reading... 'The staff were panicked that the President might do this or that.' There is no evidence for the panic; it's not in the quote." (Ep 208)

**Application -- NYT Mole Op-Ed (Ep 215):**
> "Think about the things that the op-ed said were facts, and then think about how they characterize them... 'He's unhinged, he's flipping out, he's becoming unstable, he's obsessed.' What do all of those words have in common? They are things you can't know about another person's inner state." (Ep 215)

Adams proposes a clean test: separate the facts (who did what, who said what) from the characterizations (what someone was allegedly thinking or feeling). The characterizations are the mind-reading. Everything that sounds damning in the Woodward book and the NYT mole piece is characterization, not fact.

**Episodes:** 188, 190, 205, 208, 211, 215

### 8. Insanity Inversion

**Core Mechanism:** When someone behaves in a way that looks "crazy" to one group but is instrumentally effective, the question inverts: is the actor crazy, or is the observer's model of reality wrong?

**Key Quote:**
> "If you're watching somebody do something that looks insane, and yet reality keeps conforming to the insane person's plan, at what point do you start wondering if maybe the model you're using to evaluate the situation is the thing that's broken?" (Ep 194)

**Application -- Trump's "Unhinged" Behavior (Ep 211):**
> "If you put me in that job, in charge of the well-being of the whole country, and then my underlings don't want to do what I tell them to do -- what I got elected to do -- what am I going to do to them?... I am going to be so unhinged that he's going to report that I'm unhinged. And you know what I'm going to do to the next one that doesn't do what I want? I'm going to get even more unhinged. I'm going to get as unhinged as you freaking need me to be, because I'm in charge of the country." (Ep 211)

**Episodes:** 190, 194, 211

### 9. Hoax Funnel

**Core Mechanism:** A sensational claim enters the news cycle at maximum volume, then narrows progressively as facts emerge, until it either collapses to nothing or becomes a much smaller story than originally presented. Adams tracks this pattern across multiple news cycles.

**Key Quote:**
> "Every one of these 'bombshell' stories has the same pattern. It comes in big and loud and scary, and by the time you have the facts, it shrinks down to nothing." (Ep 185, paraphrased from context)

**See: Hoax Funnel Examples section below.**

**Episodes:** 185, 191, 195, 215

### 10. Negative Space Analysis

**Core Mechanism:** What is *absent* from a story is often more informative than what is present. Adams calls this "looking at the canvas and finding the empty space."

**Key Quote:**
> "Look for the blank space. Look at the canvas and find the empty space. Sometimes that's the story." (Ep 215)

**Application -- Woodward Book and Russia (Ep 215):**
> "Did Woodward leave Russia out of the book, or did he write about it in a way that didn't add anything?... Was anything added to the story by Woodward? That is the question." (Ep 215)

**Application -- NYT Mole and Russia (Ep 215):**
> "Why is it that the insider, the person who's so close to the President he can touch his desk, is not afraid of any Russian collusion stuff? Apparently, you'd think that person would have said something like, 'Well, I like how the economy is going, but I can't work for someone who's a Russian asset.' Because the insider apparently isn't worried about that." (Ep 215)

**Episodes:** 215

### 11. Complexity as Cover

**Core Mechanism:** Whenever you encounter a system of extreme complexity (algorithms, healthcare regulations, tax code), someone is getting screwed. Complexity makes accountability impossible.

**Key Quote:**
> "Remember that whenever you see complexity, somebody is getting screwed. That's one of those universal laws. Somebody's getting screwed because they can't penetrate the complexity to know they're getting screwed." (Ep 204)

**Application -- Tech Algorithms (Ep 204, 209):**
> "As long as the algorithms are complicated -- and I think that will always be the case -- the public will never have any control over them." (Ep 204)

**Application -- Healthcare (Ep 205, 210):**
Adams applies this to healthcare reform proposals: if a voter cannot evaluate whether a policy change is good or bad because it requires expert knowledge, complexity is functioning as a barrier to democratic accountability.

**Episodes:** 204, 205, 209, 210

### 12. Systems vs. Goals (Applied to Prediction)

**Core Mechanism:** Goals are discrete desired outcomes ("I want healthcare for everybody"). Systems are repeatable processes that improve your odds over time. Adams argues that systems thinkers make better predictions because they evaluate processes, not endpoints.

**Key Quote:**
> "Does it seem to you that Democrats are goal thinkers and Republicans are systems thinkers? When Democrats talk about healthcare, they say, 'I want healthcare for everybody.' That's a goal. I would like that too, except I don't know any system that would get you there." (Ep 201)

**Application -- Kanye West (Ep 203):**
Adams uses Kanye as a case study of systems thinking in action: talk to anyone (system), stack talents (system), lead with love (system), take risks with unlimited upside and limited downside (system). Each is contrasted with the "goal" approach of Black Lives Matter.

**Episodes:** 201, 203

### 13. The "Pushing on Every Door" Leadership Model

**Core Mechanism:** Trump's leadership style is to challenge every assumption, not because he believes every assumption is wrong, but to verify which ones hold. The people around him who interpret this as "he doesn't understand the issues" are confusing probing with ignorance.

**Key Quote:**
> "You're seeing a president who simply pushes every door. He just doesn't accept that the old way of thinking or doing is automatically right... The fact that you're pushing on every door is not a sign of bad leadership." (Ep 209)

**Application -- Nuclear Question:**
> "If you made me president, I might ask that question just to make sure that I understand why it can never be done." (Ep 209)

**Episodes:** 209, 211

### 14. Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD) Applied to Kompromat

**Core Mechanism:** The standard "Putin has blackmail on Trump" framework fails because it ignores the MAD dynamic. If Putin released compromising material (real or fabricated), the US would retaliate against Putin personally. The framework applies to all major-power leaders, not just Trump.

**Key Quote:**
> "Does Putin have blackmail material about Trump? Let me give you the definitive answer to that with no doubt whatsoever: yes. Or he can invent it, and the public will treat it the same... For all practical purposes, he has something as good as blackmail material. Now, if this were not President Trump and it was another leader, would it be different?" (Ep 207)

> "If Putin has something on the President, or he decided to make up something and act as if it were true, in both cases he would be initiating mutually assured destruction." (Ep 207)

**Episodes:** 196, 207

### 15. Facts-to-Fiction Conversion (The Belushi Method)

**Core Mechanism:** All facts in a story can be individually accurate while the combined story is completely misleading. Adams cites Woodward's book on John Belushi as a proven example of this pattern, then applies it to the Trump-era Woodward book.

**Key Quote:**
> "Here's the freaky thing: the facts that Woodward reported about Belushi were accurate, but when it was put together by Woodward, it was completely different from actual reality. Those two things can happen. All the facts can be right, but because of the way he combined them, it changed entirely the nature of the situation." (Ep 209)

> "It is ordinary to take real facts and turn them into the opposite of what they mean without changing the facts. You can do that just by the context, the timing, the way you frame it." (Ep 209)

**Episodes:** 209

### 16. The Narcissist Profile (Applied to Mole Hunt)

**Core Mechanism:** Adams builds a behavioral profile of the NYT anonymous op-ed author using prediction logic: (a) believes their judgment is superior to the President's without evidence, (b) wants fame but not accountability, (c) is unlikely to let anyone rewrite their prose. This profile narrows the suspect pool.

**Key Quote:**
> "If it wasn't political and it wasn't a Hillary supporter and it isn't consistent with really trying to help the world, what does it look like? It looks like somebody who wanted to be famous and felt that they were too important to get in trouble." (Ep 213)

> "Does a narcissist let somebody else rewrite their writing so that it is less identifiable as the person who originally wrote it?... I've got a feeling that whoever wrote that very much wanted their writing to be the thing that was in the New York Times." (Ep 213)

**Episodes:** 211, 213

### 17. Visual Persuasion Dominance

**Core Mechanism:** Visual information overwhelms all other forms of evidence in shaping public opinion. A single compelling image defeats a thousand words of context.

**Key Quote (applied to Elon Musk):**
> "His stock probably went down because what he did on Joe Rogan gave people a visual image that got passed around a lot that reminded them of the other stories about him... the one that was completely unimportant had a visual element. You can see that the visual element again overwhelms every other element." (Ep 212)

**Episodes:** 212

### 18. The Flip-Flop as Strategic Probe

**Core Mechanism:** What critics call "flip-flopping" is actually a systems-level testing process: propose an idea, measure pushback, adjust. Adams defends this as superior to rigid adherence to initial positions.

**Key Quote:**
> "It is my way as well to toss out ideas and see how they're received. If people come back to me with pushback and it's something I haven't thought of before, I'm going to flip-flop... I'd be more worried about somebody who said, 'Here's the decision. You have new information? Doesn't matter. I made the decision.'" (Ep 211)

**Episodes:** 211

## Track Record Discussions

### 1. Manafort Verdict Self-Evaluation (Ep 191)
Adams predicted the jury would have difficulty reaching a unanimous verdict on all counts. When Manafort was convicted on 8 of 18 counts with the jury hung on 10, Adams claimed vindication:
> "I said look for something like a hung jury or a split... That's pretty close to what I predicted." (Ep 191)

### 2. Peak TDS Premature Call (Ep 187)
Adams acknowledges a timing error on his "Peak TDS" prediction:
> "I predicted that we'd hit peak Trump Derangement Syndrome about a year ago, and I missed it by about a year. I was premature." (Ep 187)
This is a notable instance of Adams publicly acknowledging a failed prediction.

### 3. Trade Domino Effect (Ep 197-198)
Adams predicted doing all trade deals simultaneously from a position of economic strength would create a domino effect. When Mexico appeared to reach a deal and Canada began negotiating:
> "It only took 24 hours for me to see the first story of all the people who say the Mexico deal is actually bad for the United States. You knew that was coming." (Ep 198)

### 4. 2015 Trump Win Prediction Revisited (Ep 211)
Adams references his original 2015 prediction framework:
> "Do you remember when I told you in 2015 that facts wouldn't matter but persuasion would? Everybody kept saying, 'You're crazy, facts matter.'" (Ep 211)

### 5. Cuban Embassy Mass Hysteria (Ep 205)
Adams updates his prediction with a refined theory:
> "My initial take on that was that it was probably a mass hysteria... the bigger part of my prediction of mass hysteria is that however many people were actually affected by a real thing, I'm going to say there are more people imagining that they had effects than there are people who actually have it." (Ep 205)

### 6. Midterms Prediction Clarification (Ep 212)
Adams pushes back against claims he predicted a "Red Wave":
> "Am I still predicting a Red Wave? I've never predicted a Red Wave. My only prediction was nine months ago: that the midterms would be closer than what people thought back in January." (Ep 211)

### 7. North Korea Directional Assessment (Ep 215)
Adams frames his NK prediction methodology:
> "Things are moving very slowly, but very clearly in the right direction. I don't think anything is going in reverse." (Ep 215)

### 8. Analysis Credibility Meta-Discussion (Ep 207)
Adams addresses critics who say he is "destroying his analysis credibility":
> "People have been saying this about me since I started talking in public. It's the most common complaint: 'This time, I got it all wrong. You've jumped the shark this time.'" (Ep 207)

## Hoax Funnel Examples

### 1. Cohen/Manafort "Beginning of the End" (Ep 191-192)
**Initial Claim:** Cohen's plea deal and Manafort's conviction mean Trump will be impeached; "worse than Watergate."
**Funnel Progression:** Cohen cooperated but produced no evidence of Russian collusion. Manafort convicted on tax/bank fraud unrelated to Trump campaign.
**Adams's Framing:**
> "These stories that are coming out today are going to seem completely different in a week... Wait for the facts to come in." (Ep 191)

### 2. Mob Boss Frame (Ep 195)
**Initial Claim:** Trump behaves like a mob boss; pattern of obstruction proves guilt.
**Funnel Application:** Adams applies the Bigfoot Test -- the "mob boss" pattern is indistinguishable from the pattern of a person who is not guilty but is frustrated with an investigation.
> "Notice what they did. They said there's no evidence of any crime, and then they described the pattern as being consistent with a mob boss." (Ep 195)

### 3. Woodward Book Expectations (Ep 208-209, 215)
**Initial Claim:** Woodward's investigative credibility means his book will be the definitive case against Trump.
**Funnel Progression:** Key sources (Mattis, Kelly, Dowd) denied quotes. No new Russia revelations. Adams calls it "the poor man's Fire and Fury -- too little, too late."
**Adams's Framing:**
> "His sources may have actually said that, and he might actually trust his sources, but as you see the denials coming in, the denials sound fairly credible as well." (Ep 208)

### 4. NYT Anonymous Op-Ed as Structural Threat (Ep 211-215)
**Initial Claim:** An anonymous insider confirms the President is mentally unfit and cabinet members considered the 25th Amendment.
**Funnel Progression:** The 25th Amendment discussion was early and abandoned. The "worst" allegation was that staff sometimes delayed or redirected presidential decisions -- normal in any organization. The mole's identity (Miles Taylor) turned out to be a mid-level official.
**Adams's Framing:**
> "This is the closest thing to a genuine insider who really knows where the bodies are buried, and all he had was his own interpretation of what somebody else's thoughts are. That was the worst of it." (Ep 215)

## Teaching Sequences

### Sequence 1: How to Evaluate a News Story (Ep 208-211)
1. Separate facts from characterizations (Ep 208, 215)
2. Identify mind-reading (claims about someone's inner state) (Ep 208)
3. Apply the Bigfoot Test (is the observer adding a pattern?) (Ep 194-195)
4. Check for facts-to-fiction conversion (all facts true but story misleading) (Ep 209)
5. Look for the negative space (what is conspicuously absent?) (Ep 215)
6. Wait 48 hours for clarification (Ep 185, 208)

### Sequence 2: How to Evaluate a Prediction (Ep 184-191)
1. Define directional vs. factual accuracy (Ep 196)
2. Identify which pattern is more predictive (Ep 197)
3. Check if the prediction requires mind-reading (Ep 190)
4. Apply the Bigfoot Test to the evidence (Ep 194)
5. Evaluate track record of the predictor (Ep 211)
6. Look for premature evaluation (Ep 187 -- "I missed it by about a year")

### Sequence 3: How to Counter-Persuade on Policy (Ep 205, 208)
1. Pace first: agree with the other side's goal (Ep 208)
2. Challenge the system, not the goal (Ep 201)
3. Don't use labels ("socialist") as arguments (Ep 205)
4. Offer a concrete alternative system (Ep 208)
5. Lead with the outcome number (Ep 210 -- "$800 to $200")

### Sequence 4: Profiling an Anonymous Source (Ep 211-213)
1. Analyze writing style (stylometry) (Ep 211)
2. Estimate gender from prose style (Ep 211)
3. Evaluate ego signature (narcissist test: would they let someone rewrite?) (Ep 213)
4. Map motivations: fame vs. ideology vs. genuine concern (Ep 213)
5. Triangulate via social connections (lie detector on associates) (Ep 211)

# Progression of Awareness

Adams maps a four-stage progression of political and persuasion awareness. Most people never get past Stage 1. The persuasion-aware person operates at Stage 4.

## The Four Stages

From [[2018-06-14-XqoXXwTSgeo-episode-90|Episode 90]]:

### Stage 1: "The Other Side Is Evil"
The default. You disagree with the other side because they are bad people — stupid, evil, corrupt, or malicious. This is where most political discussion operates. It requires no self-examination and produces no insight.

### Stage 2: "The Other Side Has Different Information"
Slightly better. You recognize an information gap — they would agree with you if they knew what you know. This is the academic/journalist frame: the problem is ignorance, and the solution is more facts. Adams considers this stage only marginally better than Stage 1 because it still assumes your filter is correct and theirs is a deficiency.

### Stage 3: "Both Sides Are Seeing Different Movies"
The [[two-movies-on-one-screen|two movies]] framework. You understand that perception itself differs — same facts, different interpretations. This is a major leap because it requires accepting that your movie might not be the accurate one. Most people who reach this stage intellectually still feel certain their movie is correct.

### Stage 4: "I Can Spot My Own Triggers"
Master level. You recognize when your own [[cognitive-dissonance|cognitive dissonance]] is being activated and can compensate. You watch yourself having emotional reactions to information and ask: "Is this my rational assessment or is this a triggered response?" You don't trust your certainty.

## Why Most People Stay at Stage 1

Adams connects stage progression to [[ego-as-tool|ego]]: each stage requires more ego flexibility than the last.

- Stage 1 requires no ego flexibility (the other side is wrong; I'm right)
- Stage 2 requires minimal flexibility (they're not evil, just uninformed)
- Stage 3 requires significant flexibility (my perception might be equally constructed)
- Stage 4 requires radical flexibility (my emotional certainty is a tell, not a guide)

Since most people treat ego as identity rather than as a tool, they cannot reach Stages 3 and 4 without feeling like they're losing themselves.

# Audience of One

Adams's principle that persuasion must be designed for its actual target, not for the audience watching from the sidelines. When critics mock a persuasion attempt as crude, embarrassing, or unsophisticated, they are often revealing that they don't understand who the message was designed for.

## The Singapore Video

Adams's canonical example is Trump's video for Kim Jong-un at the Singapore Summit. American media ridiculed it as "cheesy" and "propagandistic." Adams's analysis from [[2018-06-13-5fsFaAvBaAc-episode-103|Episode 103]]: "President Trump did not make a video to show to The New Yorker magazine... He made a video for one guy."

The video's style matched North Korean propaganda aesthetics because that's what the audience — Kim Jong-un — responds to. Adams: "Who else said, 'Of course it looks weird to us, it's not for us'? Do you ever watch a children's cartoon on TV and you say to yourself, 'This children's cartoon does not appeal to me'? Do you know why? It wasn't made for you."

The critics committed a specific analytical error: evaluating the persuasion as if THEY were the target. From their lens (sophisticated American media consumers), the video was crude. From the actual target's lens (a North Korean dictator raised on state propaganda), the video was perfectly calibrated.

## The Broader Principle

Adams uses the audience-of-one framework to explain why expert analysis of persuasion often fails: analysts evaluate persuasion quality against their own preferences rather than against the actual target's psychology. A college professor evaluating a political rally misses the persuasion because rallies aren't designed for college professors.

This connects directly to [[two-movies-on-one-screen|two movies]]: the media watched one movie ("the president made an embarrassing amateur video") while the actual diplomatic dynamic was a completely different movie ("the president gave Kim exactly the kind of presentation that would move him").

## Application

Before judging any communication as "bad":
1. **Identify the actual audience.** Who is this FOR?
2. **Evaluate against their frame,** not yours
3. **If it looks wrong to you,** consider whether you might not be the audience
4. **If the actual target responds positively,** the communication worked — regardless of what observers think

# Failing Forward

Adams's subtitle says it all: *How to Fail at Almost Everything and Still Win Big*. The claim: failure is not the opposite of success — it is the raw material. Each failure, within a system, adds skills, contacts, knowledge, and experience that make subsequent attempts more likely to succeed.

The key distinction: failure within a GOAL framework is defeat. Failure within a SYSTEM framework is an investment. The goal-setter who fails feels like a loser. The system-runner who fails has new data.

## Adams's Failure Resume

Adams uses his own career as the primary evidence:

Before Dilbert succeeded, Adams failed at:
- A computer game (learned about technology)
- A food-related business (learned about retail)
- A meditation-instruction business (learned about marketing)
- Attempts at television (learned about media)
- Various other entrepreneurial ventures (learned about business)

Each failure added to his [[talent-stack|talent stack]]. By the time he created Dilbert, he had accumulated skills in technology, business, marketing, media, and office culture — none of them learned through deliberate career planning, all of them learned through failing at something else.

Adams: "I didn't plan Dilbert. I had a system of trying new things until something worked. Each failure taught me something or introduced me to someone."

## The System Test

Adams's test for whether a failure is productive: "If the specific project fails, did the system still move you forward?" If you tried a business and it failed but you learned sales, made connections, and identified a market gap — the system worked even though the project failed.

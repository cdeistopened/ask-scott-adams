# Shake the Box

Adams's term for expanding the variable space in a stuck negotiation or problem. When two parties are deadlocked on a binary question (yes/no, your way/my way), the solution is not to argue harder — it is to add variables until new combinations create solutions that didn't exist in the original frame.

## The Mechanism

From [[2018-06-17-episode-76|Episode 76]]: "He goes into a situation... and the first thing he does is he shakes the box. And then things land in new places, and that's either good for him or he shakes the box again... He has this superpower where simply shaking the box and moving the variables around until they favor him kind of works every time."

The critical insight comes from [[2018-07-12-LM3dqOGGGPM-episode-137|Episode 137]], where Adams introduces the slot machine analogy: "It's sort of like what I call the slot machine that you don't have to put money into. Imagine you went to Vegas and you sit in front of a slot machine and, for some reason, it's broken and it won't take your money but it will pay out... How often will you win? 100% of the time, so long as nothing stops you from continuing to pull."

The precondition: "If you're the only person in the conversation who can pick up the box and shake it, how often will you win?... A hundred percent." The box-shaker wins 100% of the time because each shake is a free lottery ticket.

## Taking the Heat

Adams identifies the prerequisite for shaking the box: willingness to absorb the consequences. From [[2018-07-13-episode-144|Episode 144]]: "President Trump can take the heat like nobody you've ever seen before. You can't shake the box unless you're willing to put up with whatever happens." Most people cannot shake the box because the short-term discomfort of randomized variables is psychologically unbearable. The box-shaker's advantage is not intellectual — it's emotional tolerance.

## Adams's Primary Case Study: North Korea

The nuclear negotiation was stuck in a binary: "give up nukes" vs. "keep nukes." Adams observed Trump expanding the variable space:

Instead of nukes-or-no-nukes, the negotiation included: economic development, security guarantees, personal relationship between leaders, international prestige, tourism potential, real estate development, diplomatic recognition, sanctions relief, military exercise reduction, and more.

Each new variable created new trade possibilities. "Nukes for sanctions relief" is one deal. "Nukes for economic development + security guarantees + personal prestige + tourism + real estate" is a fundamentally different — and potentially more attractive — deal.

## Limits

Adams acknowledges that shaking the box works for ideas but not for physical infrastructure. You can add negotiation variables infinitely. You cannot easily reorganize physical geography or built systems. "Trump shakes ideas, not infrastructure."

## Application Beyond Diplomacy

The principle applies everywhere:
- **Salary negotiation:** Stuck on base salary? Add variables: equity, title, flexibility, start date, review timeline, bonus structure.
- **Product design:** Binary feature debate? Add user segments, implementation phases, measurement criteria.
- **Relationships:** Binary conflict? Add variables: timing, context, underlying needs, third-party perspectives.

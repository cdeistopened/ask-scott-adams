# Analogies Don't Persuade

Adams's most counterintuitive persuasion claim: analogies — which feel like the strongest argument to the person using them — are actually the worst persuasion tool. They backfire almost every time.

From Episode 107: "Is there anything analogies can do except make things worse? Whenever anybody uses an analogy in a persuasive context, it just totally blows up. It doesn't do anything good."

## Why They Fail

The failure mechanism is specific: when you present an analogy, the audience's brain automatically searches for differences between the analogy and the real situation. Finding differences feels like "winning" the argument, even if the underlying point was valid.

"He's literally Hitler" invites the audience to list all the ways the person is NOT Hitler — concentration camps, genocide, world war — and each difference feels like a refutation of the original point. The analogy hands ammunition to the opposition.

## What Works Instead

Adams argues that direct visual imagery does what analogies try to do, without the vulnerability:

- **Analogy (weak):** "The border situation is like putting children in prison camps."
- **Visual (strong):** Show an actual image of children in actual facilities.

The visual bypasses the analogy-critique circuit entirely. There is no comparison to pick apart. There is only the image and the emotional response.

## The Magical Thinking Connection

Adams groups analogies with two other forms of thinking that feel rational but aren't:

1. **Pattern recognition** applied to human behavior (seeing patterns that may not exist)
2. **"History repeats itself"** claims (cherry-picking historical parallels)
3. **Analogies** (substituting comparison for evidence)

All three feel like analysis. All three are, in Adams's framing, forms of magical thinking.

# Mind Reading Fallacy

Adams identifies "mind reading" as the single most common and most destructive thinking error in public discourse. The pattern: attributing secret motives to someone without evidence, then arguing against the attributed motive rather than the person's stated position or observable actions.

From [[2018-06-14-ME7pxqlP0_8-episode-81|Episode 81]]: "We think we're good at knowing people's intentions, but we're terrible at it. Have you ever been in a relationship? You know what I'm talking about."

## Adams's Rule

"Tell me what they DID, not why they did it." Actions are observable. Motives are invented.

Adams's standard challenge: when someone claims to know another person's secret motivation, ask for the evidence. Not "it stands to reason" or "obviously they're trying to..." — observable evidence that the attributed motive is the actual motive.

His observation: this request almost always produces silence, indignation, or a circular argument ("You can just tell" or "Everyone knows that's what they really think").

## The Mind Reading Tells

Adams identifies mind-reading in public discourse through these patterns:

- "What they REALLY mean is..."
- "The real reason they're doing this is..."
- "They're just doing this because..."
- "Deep down, they know that..."
- "This is really about..."

Each attributes an unobservable internal state as if it were established fact.

## Exception

Adams acknowledges that actions sometimes make motives obvious: "If somebody takes a knife and stabs somebody else a thousand times and you say, 'I think he meant to kill the person,' well, that's not really mind-reading because that's more like a description of what just happened."

The line: inferring motives from actions is reasonable. Inferring motives independent of or contradicting actions is mind reading.

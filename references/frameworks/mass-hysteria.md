# Mass Hysteria

Adams's most controversial diagnostic: mass hysteria is a state where a large population shares a belief in a narrative that is demonstrably false, and the belief persists — even strengthens — despite exposure to contradicting evidence. Adams explicitly uses the clinical term "hallucination" to describe this state, which is deliberately provocative.

His most prominent claim: the reaction to Trump's 2016 election produced "the biggest mass hysteria event in human history." This is characteristically hyperbolic and impossible to verify — but Adams defends the framing by noting that the scale of the triggered population, the intensity of the cognitive dissonance, and the duration of the event were historically unusual.

## The Mechanism

Adams describes mass hysteria as a viral phenomenon:

1. **A trigger event** shatters a widely held belief (e.g., Trump winning when virtually everyone predicted he couldn't)
2. **Cognitive dissonance** activates at scale — millions of people simultaneously experience the discomfort of having their worldview proven wrong
3. **Rather than update**, the population constructs a new narrative that preserves the old worldview (e.g., "the election was stolen by Russia" preserves the belief that Trump couldn't have won legitimately)
4. **Social reinforcement** makes the new narrative viral — people in cognitive dissonance seek each other out, creating echo chambers that reinforce the shared hallucination
5. **The narrative becomes self-sustaining** — contradicting evidence is interpreted as further evidence for the conspiracy

Adams: "Hallucinations are viral. Once a critical mass of people share the same hallucination, it becomes socially reinforced. Each person's belief confirms the others'. The bigger the group, the stronger the hallucination."

## Adams's Diagnostic Criteria

Adams distinguishes mass hysteria from ordinary disagreement using several criteria:

1. **A clear trigger** — an event that shattered a previously held certain belief
2. **Rapid narrative construction** — a new explanation appears quickly and is adopted without evidence
3. **Immunity to debunking** — evidence against the narrative is reinterpreted as evidence for it
4. **Escalation** — the claims get more extreme over time, not less
5. **Tells of cognitive dissonance** — one-variable explanations, mind reading, absurd absolutes (see [[cognitive-dissonance|cognitive dissonance tells]])
6. **Prediction failure** — the hysteria-driven predictions consistently fail, but confidence doesn't decrease

## The Controversy

This is Adams's most politically charged framework. His primary application — diagnosing anti-Trump reaction as mass hysteria — is inherently political. Critics argue:

- Adams only applies the "mass hysteria" label to one side of the political spectrum
- The diagnosis can be used to dismiss legitimate concerns as psychological pathology
- It is unfalsifiable from the inside (anyone in "mass hysteria" would deny it, which Adams frames as confirmation)

Adams's defense: he applies the diagnostic criteria consistently. A mass hysteria diagnosis requires a specific trigger, rapid unfounded narrative construction, immunity to debunking, and prediction failure. If those criteria are met, the diagnosis applies regardless of which political tribe is affected.

Whether you find this convincing depends largely on which movie you're watching — which Adams would note is itself evidence for the [[two-movies-on-one-screen|two movies]] framework.

## The Wiki's Position

This wiki documents Adams's frameworks as thinking tools. The mass hysteria framework can be applied analytically without adopting Adams's specific political conclusions. The diagnostic criteria — trigger, rapid narrative, immunity to debunking, escalation, prediction failure — are observable and testable regardless of the analyst's political position.

The question "is this mass hysteria?" is distinct from "is Adams right about which side is hallucinating?" The former is analytical. The latter is political.

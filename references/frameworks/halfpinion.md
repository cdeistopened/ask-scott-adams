# Halfpinion

Adams coined "halfpinion" to describe a specific form of weak argument: a complaint that offers no alternative. A full opinion compares two options: "I prefer X over Y, because..." A halfpinion states only half: "Y is bad." Without the comparison, the complaint is analytically worthless — everything looks bad compared to perfection, and everything looks good compared to the worst alternative.

From Episode 107: "A regular opinion is when you say, 'I prefer this situation over this situation.' A full opinion is, 'I've looked at all the alternatives. We could go this way, we could go this way; I prefer this one over this one.' That's an opinion... A halfpinion is when you say half of that."

## The Missing "And"

Adams identified a specific variant — the complaint that lists a negative fact but omits the consequence:

"When you look at the complaints from the anti-Trumpers, they're all missing the 'and.' A typical anti-Trumper will say, 'The President has lied 3,600 times since he came into office.' And? 'And that killed people and hurt the economy.' They always leave out the 'and.'"

If the "and" (the actual negative consequence) cannot be specified, the complaint is a halfpinion masquerading as analysis.

## Diagnostic Use

When evaluating any criticism — political, professional, or personal — ask: "Compared to what?" If the critic cannot specify the alternative they prefer, or cannot specify the actual negative consequence, they are offering a halfpinion.

# Cognitive Dissonance

Adams uses "cognitive dissonance" differently from the psychology textbook definition. In Festinger's original formulation, cognitive dissonance is the discomfort of holding contradictory beliefs. In Adams's usage, it is the state people enter when something they were certain about is shown to be completely wrong — and instead of updating their model, they rewrite reality to protect the old belief.

Adams's version is more specific and more actionable: he provides a diagnostic framework of observable "tells" that indicate someone has crossed from rational disagreement into cognitive dissonance. The canonical teaching episode is [[2018-06-14-ME7pxqlP0_8-episode-81|Episode 81: "How to Spot Cognitive Dissonance"]], where he walks through each tell with examples.

## The Trigger

Cognitive dissonance requires a trigger — a moment when something you believed to be true is shown to be definitively, unambiguously wrong. Without a trigger, there is no dissonance.

Adams on the trigger: "You don't really get cognitive dissonance unless there was something you believed to be true that has been shown for sure to be untrue."

His primary example: November 9th, 2016. For people who were certain Trump could not win, the election result was a trigger of unprecedented scale. Their model of how the world worked — their intelligence, their ability to read people, their understanding of politics — was obliterated in a single night.

Adams: "Everything they thought they understood about their own intelligence, their own ability to see what's happening, their own capability to predict — all destroyed. Just obliterated. That's a trigger. That's a big trigger. It's the biggest trigger we've ever seen."

## The Diagnostic Tells

Adams's contribution is not the concept of cognitive dissonance (which is well-established) but the set of observable tells that indicate it. These are the diagnostic markers he teaches for identifying when someone — including yourself — has left rational analysis.

### Tell 1: One-Variable Explanation

Collapsing a complex, multi-factor situation into a single explanation.

Adams's example from Episode 81: When Kanye West said people should think more freely, critics responded with "He's just crazy" or "He's just trying to sell records." One variable. No consideration of multiple simultaneous motivations.

"If you hear somebody go through all the variables, they're probably not in cognitive dissonance. But if somebody fixates on one, that doesn't tell you they're in cognitive dissonance; it's just your first flag."

### Tell 2: Absurd Absolute

Taking a perfectly reasonable point and expanding it to a ridiculous extreme.

Adams: "Suppose Kanye says, 'I think we should be more open in our thinking.' Imagine somebody listens to that and they go, 'Yeah, now you're gonna let children drive cars?'"

A reasonable suggestion inflated to an absurd conclusion is "pretty solid — you see this one, you usually don't have to look much further."

### Tell 3: Mind Reading

Attributing secret motives without evidence. "What they REALLY mean is..." or "The real reason is..."

Adams: "We think we're good at knowing people's intentions, but we're terrible at it. Have you ever been in a relationship? You know what I'm talking about."

His rule: "If you're judging it by their actions, then you're probably pretty close. But if you see somebody doing some just pure mind-reading that doesn't seem connected to the actions that are being observed, that's a tell."

### Tell 4: Word Salad

Responses that use many words — often bigger words than necessary — but don't actually say anything falsifiable.

"Arguments that have a lot of words; they're often bigger words than you need, they seem to be too many of them, they're kind of conceptual, and when you're done reading it, you say, 'Okay, what? I'm not even sure what that meant.'"

### Tell 5: Unspecific Doom Forecast

Vague catastrophic predictions that sound alarming but lack specificity.

Adams: "The person who says, 'Well, if this keeps going the way it's going, it could tear apart the fabric of civilization.' What does that hurt? I understand what you're saying, but did that make me lose money? Did it hurt? Could I not find love in this world?"

### Tell 6: Ignoring Prediction Track Record

The person's model has been proven wrong, but they continue speaking with unchanged confidence.

Adams: "If my predictions suddenly stop working and you see me stick with my same explanation of reality... I've been predicting now in public for two years better than anybody."

### Tell 7: Verbal Tells

Specific words and patterns that broadcast cognitive dissonance:

- **"So..."** — "So you're saying..." almost always precedes a strawman (Absurd Absolute)
- **"Bro" / "Dude"** — When the word IS the argument, not a casual address
- **"Ahahahaha"** — Laughter without reason. "You left out the reason" is Adams's standard response.

### Extended: The Laundry List Tell

Adams developed the laundry list tell into one of his most detailed analyses. From [[2018-06-16-nwCL1gTZ2F8-episode-106|Episode 106]]: "The anti-Trumpers who will list quite often five things... The first thing I should say is I'm not relying on science for this. As far as I know, there's no scientific study that would support what I'm going to tell you... My thinking is that the people who have decided they dislike the President started with big reasons in the beginning... It's now been 520 days of the presidency, and he has made nuclear war far less likely... So all of the big stuff that people had in their heads as 'these are the reasons we hate Trump' — the big, big things — they just don't work anymore."

When pressed, they stack weak complaints: "con man" + "liar" + "cult of personality" + "Russia" + "children in cages" = "BAM, Hitler!" The diagnostic value: when someone needs five reasons where one strong reason would suffice, none of the five is actually the reason. The real reason is emotional, and the list is post-hoc rationalization.

## The Hard Part

Adams's most honest admission: "If you're the one who's experiencing it, you can't tell. The odds are just as good if you don't know anything else about the situation."

The diagnostic tells are more reliable for observing others than yourself. The one self-diagnostic Adams trusts: check for the trigger. "You look to see who had a trigger — something they believed to be true that has been shown for sure to be untrue."

If your worldview has been confirmed by events, the odds of YOUR cognitive dissonance are lower (though not zero). If your worldview was just shattered, the odds are high.

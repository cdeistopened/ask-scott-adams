# Affirmation System

Adams claims to have used a specific affirmation technique for decades: writing a specific desired outcome 15 times per day, by hand. He attributes several major life outcomes to this practice — including the creation of Dilbert and his GMAT score — while explicitly acknowledging that he cannot explain the mechanism and cannot distinguish between mystical causation and focused attention.

## The Method

1. Write a specific affirmation 15 times per day
2. Write by hand (Adams is specific — typing doesn't count)
3. Use first person, present or future tense
4. Be specific about the outcome
5. Do it daily without fail

**Format:** "I, [Name], will [specific outcome]."

Adams's examples:
- "I, Scott Adams, will become a famous cartoonist" — written before Dilbert existed
- "I, Scott Adams, will get an MBA from Berkeley" — he was admitted
- "I, Scott Adams, will score in the 94th percentile on the GMAT" — he scored exactly 94th percentile

The GMAT story is the most striking because of its specificity. Adams does not claim to understand why he wrote 94th percentile rather than, say, 95th or 99th. He presents it as data without explanation.

## Adams's Honest Caveats

Adams is unusually transparent about the limits of his claims:

- He does not know if affirmations work through mystical means or through focused attention and priming
- He acknowledges he cannot track what he DIDN'T affirm (no control group)
- He presents it as personal experience, not as a scientifically validated practice
- He considers the risk zero: "What's the downside? You wasted 5 minutes writing?"

## Affirmation vs. Intention

Adams draws a distinction between affirmations (internal) and intention (external). From [[rumble-episode-1229|Episode 1229]], discussing Marc Benioff: "Clear, clean intentions attract everything you need to make them work." And: "An affirmation is something maybe that you're chanting or writing down... That's sort of an internal process. Intention, if you do it right, is something you put into the universe and other people see it and then they're drawn to it."

The key distinction: affirmations program the self; intentions program the environment. Both are moist robot programming, but operating on different targets.

Adams's compression of the entire framework: "What you decide will change your life. Your wants will not change your life, but what you decide will."

## The Materialist Interpretation

For those who reject mystical causation, affirmations may work through:
- **Priming:** Keeping the goal in working memory
- **Attention direction:** Noticing relevant opportunities
- **Identity reinforcement:** "I am someone who is working toward X"
- **Commitment device:** Daily action maintains commitment
- **Specificity forcing function:** The format requires defining exactly what you want

Under this interpretation, affirmations are a [[moist-robot|moist robot]] self-programming technique — a way of setting inputs to produce desired behavioral outputs.

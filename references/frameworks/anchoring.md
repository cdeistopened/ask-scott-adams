# Anchoring

Anchoring in Adams's usage is the negotiation technique of opening with a deliberately extreme position so that the eventual "compromise" lands at your actual target. The audience (or opponent) perceives the compromise as a concession and a reasonable middle ground, when in fact it was the intended destination all along.

Adams identified anchoring as one of Trump's core techniques — both in business negotiation and in political positioning.

## The Mechanism

The psychological mechanism is well-established: humans evaluate proposals relative to reference points, not in absolute terms. The first number on the table — the anchor — shapes all subsequent perception. A car priced at $50,000 makes $35,000 feel like a deal. A car priced at $25,000 makes $35,000 feel expensive. The car is the same; the anchor determines the perception.

Adams's contribution is not discovering anchoring (it's basic negotiation science) but identifying it as a master technique in political persuasion, where most analysts missed it because they were evaluating positions as policy commitments rather than as persuasion moves.

## The Trump Application

Adams's primary case study: Trump's policy positions as anchoring moves.

**Immigration:** Opening position — deport all illegal immigrants. This anchor is extreme and generates massive media coverage. The "compromise" — enhanced border enforcement, some pathway for existing residents — lands near what a conventional Republican might have proposed as their opening position. But because the anchor was deportation, enforcement-plus-pathway feels moderate.

**Muslim ban:** Opening position — ban all Muslims from entering the country. The "compromise" — enhanced vetting from specific countries — is the actual policy goal. Without the extreme anchor, enhanced vetting would have been the controversial opening position.

Adams's insight: pundits who took these opening positions literally were demonstrating [[loserthink|loserthink]] — evaluating persuasion moves as if they were policy commitments. Anyone with negotiation experience recognized the pattern immediately: the opening position is not the real position. It is the anchor that makes the real position feel like a reasonable concession.

## Anchoring in Everyday Persuasion

Adams applies the concept beyond politics:

**Salary negotiation:** If your target is $120K, open at $150K. The eventual $125K feels like a concession you gave. Open at $120K and you end at $110K.

**Project scope:** If you need 8 weeks, propose 12. The "compromise" of 9 weeks gives you buffer. Propose 8 and you end with 6.

**Pricing:** Start high. "I normally charge $X but for this situation..." The discount from the anchor feels generous. Starting at the discounted rate gives away the concession for free.

## Common Mistakes

Adams identifies two common anchoring errors:

**1. Anchoring against yourself.** Starting with your real position (or worse, a concession) as the opening. "I think the project will take about 8 weeks, maybe we could do 7 if we pushed." You have now anchored at 7. The negotiation goes down from there.

**2. Not recognizing when you're being anchored.** If someone opens with an extreme position, most people's instinct is to argue against the extreme. Adams: the correct response is to set your own anchor on the opposite extreme, creating a negotiation space centered on your actual target — not to debate the reasonableness of their anchor.

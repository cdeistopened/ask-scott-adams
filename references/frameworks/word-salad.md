# Word Salad

Adams's term for a specific type of argument that signals cognitive dissonance: it uses many words, often bigger than necessary, sounds vaguely intellectual, but upon examination says nothing falsifiable or specific.

From [[2018-06-14-ME7pxqlP0_8-episode-81|Episode 81]]: "Arguments that have a lot of words; they're often bigger words than you need, they seem to be too many of them, they're kind of conceptual, and when you're done reading it, you say, 'Okay, what? I'm not even sure what that meant.'"

## The Diagnostic

Word salad is identifiable when:
- The response is disproportionately long relative to the question
- It uses technical or academic vocabulary without necessity
- After reading it, you cannot state what specific claim was made
- It cannot be falsified because it doesn't assert anything testable
- Rereading doesn't clarify — it remains conceptual and vague

## The Caveat

Adams acknowledges the mirror problem: "You might be the one experiencing the cognitive dissonance, and your cognitive dissonance might be what's preventing you from seeing a perfectly good argument." Sometimes complex arguments require complex language. The diagnostic is probabilistic, not certain.

# Visual Persuasion

Adams rates visual persuasion as the single strongest form of influence. Humans are visual thinkers — we process images faster than words, remember images longer, and make decisions based on mental movies more than on logical arguments.

## The Hierarchy Position

In Adams's persuasion hierarchy, visual persuasion sits at the top:

1. **Visual imagery** (strongest)
2. Identity
3. Emotion
4. Social proof
5. Reciprocity
6. Reason and facts (weakest)

This ranking explains why Adams considers "Build the Wall" one of the most effective political slogans in modern history — it creates an instant mental image that no amount of policy argument can dislodge. "Comprehensive Immigration Reform" creates no image at all.

## The Kill Shot Connection

Adams's analysis of effective [[kill-shot|kill shots]] always returns to visual quality. "Low Energy Jeb" works because you can SEE a deflated, tired person. "Crooked Hillary" works because you can SEE something bent. Labels without visual quality ("Boring Bob") are weaker because they don't create a picture.

## The Auschwitz Tweet Analysis

Adams's most detailed visual persuasion breakdown comes from his analysis of General Hayden's tweet comparing immigration policy to Auschwitz. From [[2018-06-18-episode-107|Episode 107]]: "By adding the visual, the analogy is just sort of a carrier. The analogy isn't the important part; the important part was the visual. You put that visual in your head and then you have this automatic association. What the good General is doing is top-rate CIA-quality persuasion disguised as a bad analogy because the analogy is ridiculous, but the picture is gold."

The insight: even a logically terrible analogy can be persuasively devastating if it installs the right image. The audience's rational mind rejects the comparison while their visual mind has already accepted the association. This is why [[analogies-dont-persuade|analogies don't persuade]] through logic — but they sometimes persuade through the images they smuggle in.

## The Singapore Summit Video

Adams analyzed Trump's video for Kim Jong-un as "chock-full with the strongest, most well-executed persuasion that you've probably ever seen in one place." The video was pure visual persuasion — nuclear destruction contrasted with beachfront prosperity, future development imagined in glossy footage. Critics missed the technique because they evaluated it as a policy document rather than a persuasion tool. Adams: "President Trump did not make a video to show to The New Yorker magazine... He made a video for one guy."

## Application

Adams's rule: any message can be made more persuasive by making it more visual. Convert abstractions to concrete images. Convert statistics to stories. Convert arguments to mental movies.

"If your audience can't see it in their mind, you haven't persuaded them. You've just made noise."

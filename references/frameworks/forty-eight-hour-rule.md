# 48-Hour Rule

Adams's practical heuristic for information hygiene: after a controversial statement or event, wait 48 hours before forming a judgment. The initial facts are almost always incomplete, distorted, or flat wrong.

## The Mechanism

The modern media cycle rewards speed over accuracy. The first version of any controversial story is optimized for clicks, not for truth. Within 48 hours, the story almost always changes — context emerges, quotes get fuller, the narrative narrows (see [[hoax-funnel|hoax funnel]]). People who form judgments in the first 48 hours are basing their conclusions on the worst version of the information.

## Application

From Episode 276, on the Megyn Kelly controversy: "If somebody says, 'Hey, that phrase was racially offensive,' then the person who said it should have 48 hours to say, 'I didn't even know it was racially offensive. I take it back.'" Adams argues the 48-hour window should be a cultural norm, not just a personal practice.

From Episode 286, applied to the "cotton-pickin'" controversy: Adams uses the 48-Hour Rule explicitly as a framework for why rushing to judgment produces worse outcomes than patience.

## Connection to Halfpinions

The 48-Hour Rule is the operational implementation of the [[halfpinion|halfpinion]] concept. During the first 48 hours, the only intellectually honest position is a halfpinion — a provisional assessment that acknowledges incomplete information. People who commit to full opinions within the first 48 hours are engaging in what Adams considers a predictable and diagnosable thinking error.

# Thinking Past the Sale

A persuasion technique Adams identifies from both hypnosis and sales training. The concept: instead of arguing for a decision, get the audience to imagine the world AFTER the decision is made. Once they are mentally living in the post-decision reality, the decision itself feels like a foregone conclusion.

## The Mechanism

The brain does not sharply distinguish between imagining something and remembering it. When you imagine living in a new house, your brain processes the experience similarly to actually living there. This creates a psychological attachment to the imagined outcome — and resistance to returning to the pre-decision state.

Adams's sales analogy: "A good salesperson never asks 'Do you want to buy this car?' They ask 'Do you prefer the red one or the blue one?' Both questions assume the sale is made. The second one has you already past the decision, choosing between post-purchase options."

## The Singapore Summit Video

Adams's most detailed analysis of thinking past the sale centers on the video Trump played for Kim Jong-un at the Singapore Summit. From [[2018-06-13-5fsFaAvBaAc-episode-103|Episode 103]]: the video "makes you think 'past the sale,' past peace to how good peace will be." Instead of debating whether peace was possible, viewers were already imagining beachfront condos on North Korean shoreline. The video was "chock-full with the strongest, most well-executed persuasion that you've probably ever seen in one place" — visual, thinking past the sale, contrast principle, ego as tool, and "what if" framing, all layered simultaneously.

## Adams's Political Application

Adams identified Trump's wall discussion as a textbook "thinking past the sale." By debating wall design, cost, and construction timeline, the national conversation was already past the question of WHETHER to build a wall. The debate was about HOW to build it — which assumed it would be built.

The diplomatic version from [[2018-06-16-4noxkODoz8s-episode-75|Episode 75]]: "We've decided North Korea will end up without nukes." This isn't a prediction — it's thinking past the sale applied to geopolitics. Framing denuclearization as already settled transforms the question from "whether" to "how and when."

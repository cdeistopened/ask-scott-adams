# Fake News

Adams uses "fake news" not in the political slogan sense but as an analytical category describing a specific media failure mode: stories where the headline and framing create a false impression that persists even after corrections or clarifications appear.

His thesis: fake news is not primarily about deliberate fabrication. It is about framing — the selection, emphasis, and contextualization of real facts in ways that create a false impression. The facts may be individually true while the impression they create is false.

## Adams's Taxonomy

Adams identifies several varieties:

**Omission framing:** Reporting true facts while omitting context that would reverse the interpretation. Adams's canonical example: reporting that someone "praised white supremacists" while omitting the sentence in the same speech where they explicitly condemned white supremacists.

**Headline-body divergence:** The headline creates one impression. The body of the article, if read carefully, contradicts or heavily qualifies the headline. Most people read only the headline.

**Anonymous sources architecture:** Building a narrative on unnamed sources that cannot be independently verified. The claim gains credibility through repetition and institutional authority rather than through verifiable evidence.

**The persistence problem:** Once a fake-news frame enters public consciousness, corrections cannot dislodge it. Adams connects this to [[confirmation-bias|confirmation bias]]: people who have absorbed the original frame filter all subsequent information — including corrections — through it.

## Connection to the Hoax Funnel

Fake news often feeds into the [[hoax-funnel|hoax funnel]]: a sensational (and false or misleading) claim generates enormous coverage, then narrows as facts emerge, but the original impression persists because of [[confirmation-bias|confirmation bias]] and social reinforcement.

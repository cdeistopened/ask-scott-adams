# Hoax Funnel

The hoax funnel is Adams's framework for describing how certain media narratives evolve: they begin with a sensational, alarming claim that generates enormous attention, then narrow progressively as facts emerge, until the original claim is either confirmed at scale or revealed as essentially nothing — but the original framing, emotional weight, and public belief persist regardless.

Adams tracked dozens of news stories through this pattern on Coffee with Scott Adams, noting that the funnel's stages are predictable and that most consumers stop paying attention at exactly the point where the original claim begins to collapse.

## The Three Stages

### Stage 1: The Sensational Claim
A dramatic claim enters public consciousness. It is alarming, emotionally charged, and activates strong tribal reactions. The claim is reported breathlessly and widely. Examples: "The President colluded with Russia to steal the election." "The President praised neo-Nazis."

At this stage, the claim has maximum emotional impact and minimum factual verification. The audience forms its conclusion here.

### Stage 2: The Narrowing
As investigation proceeds and facts emerge, the original claim narrows. "Collusion" becomes "contacts." "Contacts" becomes "meetings that didn't produce anything." "Praised neo-Nazis" becomes "made a statement with a specific clause that contradicted the claim." Each narrowing step is reported — but with far less coverage intensity than the original claim.

Adams's observation: the narrowing happens on page 12. The original claim happened on page 1. The audience saw page 1.

### Stage 3: The Residue
The original claim has been substantially or entirely debunked, but the emotional and narrative residue remains. People who formed their conclusions at Stage 1 never update. The label sticks. The original framing becomes permanent cultural furniture.

Adams: "The hoax funnel works because the initial claim is a kill shot — it's visual, emotional, and sticky. The correction is a fact — it's boring, complex, and forgettable."

## Adams's Diagnostic

When a new sensational claim emerges, Adams's framework suggests:

1. **Note the initial claim precisely.** Write it down. What exactly is being claimed?
2. **Watch for narrowing.** As facts emerge, is the claim holding at full strength or is it narrowing?
3. **Compare media intensity to claim substance.** If the claim has narrowed by 80% but the coverage intensity hasn't changed, the funnel is operating.
4. **Check for residue.** After the investigation concludes, do people still believe the original claim? If yes, the funnel worked.

## The Counter-Argument

Adams's critics note that not every sensational story is a hoax funnel. Some stories START big and STAY big because the original claim was accurate. Watergate was sensational and true. The Catholic Church abuse scandal was sensational and true.

Adams would agree with this objection. The hoax funnel is a diagnostic tool, not a blanket dismissal. Some funnels reveal genuine substance at the bottom. The diagnostic value is in tracking the narrowing — or lack thereof — rather than assuming the outcome in advance.

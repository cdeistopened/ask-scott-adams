# Pacing and Leading

Pacing and leading is the technique Adams learned in hypnosis training at the Hypnosis Motivation Institute and considers the foundational mechanism beneath all effective persuasion. The concept is simple: before you can redirect someone, you must first match their current state. Skip the matching step and you trigger resistance. Do the matching first and the redirect feels natural.

**Pacing:** Match the other person's current reality. Agree with them. Validate their experience. Demonstrate that you understand where they are. Build rapport by showing you are on the same wavelength.

**Leading:** Once rapport is established through pacing, redirect toward your preferred conclusion. The transition feels natural because the audience has been nodding along — their compliance momentum carries them across the bridge.

Adams: "If your opening move is to tell someone their worldview is incorrect, you've already lost. Their defenses are up. You're now arguing against a fortified position."

## The Mechanism

Adams explains pacing and leading through his hypnosis training. In clinical hypnosis, the hypnotist begins by making statements the subject will agree with — statements about the room temperature, the comfort of the chair, the sound of the voice. These are all obviously true. The subject's pattern of agreement creates a "yes set" — a psychological momentum of compliance. Once the yes set is established, the hypnotist introduces the leading suggestion, and it feels like a natural continuation rather than an imposition.

The identical structure operates in persuasion, sales, therapy, conflict resolution, and daily conversation. The difference is scale and awareness, not mechanism.

## Adams's Primary Case Study: Trump Rallies

Adams identified Trump's rally technique as the most effective pacing and leading he had ever witnessed outside a professional hypnosis context.

**The pace:** Trump would begin by matching the audience's emotional state. "The country is a disaster." "We don't win anymore." "The politicians are all talk, no action." These statements validated what the audience already felt — frustration, decline, distrust of institutions. Each statement is a pace — a demonstration that the speaker shares the audience's reality.

**The lead:** Once the audience was nodding, emotionally engaged, and feeling understood, Trump would pivot: "But we're going to start winning so much you'll get tired of winning." The lead works because it follows a long sequence of pacing. The audience is already in agreement mode. The transition from shared grievance to shared optimism feels like a natural next step rather than a demand.

Adams noted that the structure was identical to what he had learned in hypnosis training: pace, pace, pace, lead. The ratio matters — you need significantly more pacing than leading, especially when the audience starts skeptical.

## Common Mistakes

Adams identifies the most common persuasion failure as leading without pacing:

**The lecture:** Starting with your conclusion and expecting the audience to follow. "You're wrong about immigration because..." — the audience's defenses activate immediately.

**The correction:** Opening with "Actually, that's not what happened..." — the audience feels attacked before you've made your point.

**The dismissal:** "That's ridiculous. Here's what really happened..." — the audience stops listening.

All three share the same structural error: the speaker is leading without having paced. The audience has no reason to follow because the speaker has not demonstrated understanding of their current position.

## How to Pace Effectively

Adams's guidance for effective pacing:

1. **State the other person's position accurately.** Not a strawman — their actual position, ideally better than they could state it themselves.
2. **Validate the emotional content.** "I understand why you feel that way" or "That's a reasonable concern" — even if you disagree with the conclusion.
3. **Find genuine agreement.** There is almost always something in the other person's position you can honestly agree with. Start there.
4. **Match their energy level.** If they are angry, don't start calm and rational — that reads as dismissive. Acknowledge the anger, then gradually redirect the energy.

## The Seven-Word Masterclass

Adams's most compressed example of pacing and leading comes from [[2018-06-14-QrS9lRE9Ihg-episode-82|Episode 82]], where he analyzed a single sentence designed for encounters with police: "How can we stay safe today, officer?"

Seven words that layer pacing ("we're on the same team"), leading ("here's what I need"), flattery ("you're the authority"), and fear reduction in a single breath. The sentence demonstrates that expert-level pacing and leading can operate at the scale of a single sentence — you don't need a rally or a 30-minute conversation.

## Trust as Mechanism

Adams connects pacing and leading to trust-building in his analysis of Trump-Kim diplomacy. From [[2018-06-13-5fsFaAvBaAc-episode-103|Episode 103]]: "What is the very best way to receive trust? Give trust... Psychologically speaking, the very best way to get trust from someone who is reluctant to give it is to give it to them first."

Giving trust first IS pacing — it demonstrates that you share the other person's desire for a positive relationship before you lead them toward specific concessions.

## The Border Debate Example

Adams applied pacing and leading to political persuasion in real-time during the border separation debate. From Episode 106: "First, pace them. Children being separated from their parents and put in prison is the worst. It's the worst. First, agree, because you do agree... then say, 'So what should we do?' And then walk them through the options."

The instruction reveals Adams's approach to politically charged topics: lead with genuine agreement about the emotional content (pace), then redirect to practical problem-solving (lead).

## Applications Beyond Politics

Pacing and leading applies everywhere:

**Sales:** Great salespeople pace the customer's current situation and pain points before leading to their solution. Bad salespeople pitch their product immediately.

**Management:** Effective managers pace an employee's perspective before leading to a performance improvement. "I can see you've been working hard on X. Let me share some observations about Y."

**Relationships:** The most destructive conversations start with leading: "You always do X." The most productive conversations start with pacing: "I noticed we're both frustrated about X. I feel Y when it happens. What are you feeling?"

**Negotiation:** Adams connects pacing and leading directly to [[anchoring|anchoring]]. The anchor is a lead; the rapport-building is the pace. Skilled negotiators invest heavily in pacing before making their first anchoring move.

## Frameworks Found

### 1. Cognitive Dissonance (Core Framework)

**Core mechanism:** When a person's deeply held belief is contradicted by observable reality, they do not update their belief. Instead, they generate increasingly bizarre rationalizations ("word salad"), experience emotional distress, and may become hostile. Adams treats cognitive dissonance not as an occasional glitch but as the primary driver of political behavior.

**Key Quotes:**
- "One of the things that happens when people are in cognitive dissonance is they start spouting word salad and ridiculous explanations for things that don't even make sense. They don't even make sense as words." (Ep. 76)
- "A normal person, you say, 'Hey, what's your favorite color?' and the person will say, 'Hey, red. I like green.' But a person who gets triggered into cognitive dissonance -- you say to them, 'What's your favorite color?' and they go..." [freezes] (Ep. 117)
- "If you have lots of different reasons to explain something, it's probably cognitive dissonance. Not always -- there could just be lots of things -- but when you see lots of different explanations for why something unusual happened, it probably means nobody knows." (Ep. 124)
- "I predicted that people would go crazy in a way we hadn't seen since right after the election. We're seeing that now." (Ep. 111)

**Diagnostic Criteria (Cognitive Dissonance Tells):**
1. Word salad -- responses that are sentences but convey no parseable meaning
2. Multiple contradictory explanations for the same phenomenon
3. Emotional escalation without corresponding factual claims
4. Attacking the person rather than addressing the argument ("I've lost all respect for you" with no reason given)
5. Retreating to analogies rather than engaging with the actual situation
6. Freezing or going blank when confronted with disconfirming evidence

**Teaching Sequence (from Ep. 76, Spygate):**
Adams demonstrates cognitive dissonance with a four-point logical trap:
1. There was no spy in the Trump campaign
2. The spying that did not happen was totally justified
3. It would be bad for national security to identify the spy who doesn't exist
4. His name is Stefan

The contradiction between points 1-3 and point 4 creates irresolvable tension -- the hallmark of cognitive dissonance in a population.

**Episodes:** 76, 109, 111, 113, 116, 117, 124, 128, 131, 132, 137

### 2. Two Movies on One Screen

**Core mechanism:** The same set of observable facts is interpreted through completely different mental frameworks by different groups, producing two entirely incompatible narratives that both feel completely true to their holders. Adams does not claim one movie is correct; he claims the phenomenon itself is the important insight.

**Key Quotes:**
- "You're seeing very different opinions on Nielsen's performance. Some say it was amazing, some say it wasn't. But this is just your standard 'Two Movies' situation." (Ep. 110)
- "The two movies on one screen. Sometimes they don't say 'movies,' they use different ways, but the fact that we're living in two different perceived realities -- that's pretty much me, right?" (Ep. 129)
- "In one movie, people are just trying to help the country and do the best they can. But the other side, the zombie apocalypse people, think that these guys are Nazis." (Ep. 130)
- "Half the country was in some kind of mental distress because their worldview had been falsified." (Ep. 111)

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- When two groups of intelligent people look at the same event and arrive at opposite conclusions
- When adding more facts does not resolve the disagreement
- When each side cannot even articulate the other side's position accurately
- When each side believes the other side is either lying or stupid

**Relationship to other frameworks:** Two Movies is the observable symptom; cognitive dissonance is the underlying mechanism. Confirmation bias is the engine that keeps each movie running.

**Episodes:** 78, 110, 111, 121, 128, 129, 130

### 3. Analogies Are Not Thinking

**Core mechanism:** People substitute analogies for actual reasoning. An analogy is merely something that reminds you of something else -- it proves nothing, predicts nothing, and informs no decisions. Adams considers this the single most destructive cognitive error in public discourse.

**Key Quotes:**
- "How long have I been telling you that analogies are not thinking? They are just things that remind us of other things." (Ep. 111)
- "If you've made an analogy to Hitler, you're not thinking. If you made an analogy to Child Protective Services, you're not thinking." (Ep. 109)
- "An analogy is a comparison between two essentially unlike things for the purpose of definition or illustration... It can trick one into assuming that because things are similar in one respect, they must be alike in other respects." (Ep. 118, quoting a textbook)
- "Analogies are good for illustrating a point; they never prove it." (Ep. 118)
- "If you can't deal with the actual situation and you have to rely on an analogy to make your case... those are just different situations." (Ep. 109)

**The Analogy Logic Flaw (from Ep. 132):**
"If two things have something in common, you imagine that they have everything in common. That's actually just a logic flaw."

Adams applies this to:
- Detention centers compared to death camps (Ep. 111)
- Detention centers compared to kindergarten (Ep. 111 -- he mocks his own analogy)
- Detention centers compared to Child Protective Services (Ep. 109)
- White supremacists claiming credit for other white people's inventions (Ep. 132)

**Teaching Sequence (Ep. 111):**
1. Identify the analogy being used
2. Ask: "Is the system designed to help people or hurt people?" (detention center vs. death camp)
3. Note that both the pro- and anti- analogies are equally invalid
4. Return to comparing actual costs and benefits of the specific situation

**Episodes:** 109, 111, 118, 132, 137

### 4. Word-Thinking

**Core mechanism:** People mistake changing the label for changing the reality. Calling something by a different name does not change what it is or what you should do about it.

**Key Quotes:**
- "Let's not 'word-think' this and think that we've done something." (Ep. 109, on "cage" vs. "holding facility")
- "If it's your kid, it's a cage. That's the end of the story." (Ep. 109)
- "'Why are they not considered invaders?' Well, that is word-thinking. You can call them whatever you want; it doesn't change what it is. It doesn't change what you do about it. Changing the name for it doesn't change your strategy." (Ep. 109)
- "If you're telling me that theory does not equal fact, you might be an idiot... you're 'word-thinking.'" (Ep. 137, on scientific terminology)

**Relationship to other frameworks:** Word-thinking is a specific failure mode within the broader analogies-are-not-thinking framework. It also connects to Adams's persuasion work on framing -- the difference being that framing acknowledges labels change perception, while word-thinking critiques people who believe labels change reality.

**Episodes:** 109, 137

### 5. Confirmation Bias (The Master Driver)

**Core mechanism:** Once you believe something, you unconsciously filter all subsequent evidence to support that belief. Adams considers this possibly "the greatest driver of our entire perception of reality."

**Key Quotes:**
- "Confirmation bias might be the greatest driver of our entire perception of reality. Most things are probably inventions in our mind, at least things about politics." (Ep. 131)
- "If you are convinced that you've seen one or two examples of a phenomenon, then yes, you will imagine that those one or two are proof of the 500 other things that otherwise would have seemed like nothing." (Ep. 131)
- "I am so primed to believe my side that I would not be able to recognize information that disproved it." (Ep. 76)
- "Since you have a massive media that is likely to feed confirmation bias into that corruption label, it's super powerful." (Ep. 84)

**Adams's Self-Application:**
"I would not know if Russiagate was real or false, and I would not know if Spygate was real or false. It doesn't matter how much information I get, which is the freaky part." (Ep. 76)

This self-awareness is distinctive -- Adams explicitly includes himself in the confirmation bias trap, using it as a credibility move while teaching the concept.

**Cascade Effect (Ep. 131):**
The press business model primes for confirmation bias. Once a foundational story (e.g., Charlottesville) is accepted as proof, every subsequent ambiguous event gets interpreted through the same lens, creating a "cascade of confirmation bias."

**Episodes:** 76, 78, 84, 113, 118, 120, 128, 131, 132

### 6. Mass Hysteria / Trump Derangement Syndrome

**Core mechanism:** When a large enough portion of the population shares a cognitive dissonance trigger, the collective emotional response takes on a life of its own. The hysteria becomes a fact in itself -- not because the underlying belief is true, but because people's emotional state is real and must be dealt with.

**Key Quotes:**
- "This is absolutely a mass hysteria, which is separate from the real problem of children being separated. The base problem is all real, and we all care about that. But the amount of concentration on this one problem... is so far out of proportion, that it's pretty much what I expected." (Ep. 111)
- "I'm saying that there is a manufactured hysteria and that the country has fallen for it. So now it's a real thing because people are dealing with it as their top issue." (Ep. 109)
- "If somebody is crying, it doesn't help to say there's no reason to cry. You have to deal with the fact that they're crying." (Ep. 109)
- "I was talking about Trump hysteria and mass hysteria." (Ep. 129)

**Adams's Key Insight -- Hysteria as Fact:**
The manufactured nature of the hysteria does not make it less real as a political force. "The emotional argument didn't exist until recently, but now that it is here, it's a fact. The emotional argument is not imagination because the way people are feeling is the fact." (Ep. 109)

**10% Rule (Ep. 109):**
"If you have an issue that everybody is a little bit concerned about, it probably is an irrelevant issue. But if you have 10% of any public who are super emotional and care about an issue -- that moves the needle. 10% of people really, really caring changes the world."

**TDS Diagnostic (Ep. 128):**
- The losing team is more delusional because they must explain not only the facts, but why everything they predicted went wrong
- "The winning team tends to be a little less delusional than the losing team because the losing team has to explain not only the facts, but why they thought they were so smart but everything's going wrong"

**Episodes:** 109, 111, 116, 128, 129, 130

### 7. Shake the Box

**Core mechanism:** Trump's negotiation method. Take a stable situation where everyone has accepted the current arrangement of variables, grab the box, and shake it. If the new configuration isn't favorable, shake again. The key insight: if you are the only person who can shake the box, you win 100% of the time in the long run.

**Key Quotes:**
- "He goes into a situation, whether it's North Korea or the Middle East, and the first thing he does is he shakes the box. And then things land in new places, and that's either good for him or he shakes the box again." (Ep. 76)
- "If you're the only person in the conversation who can pick up the box and shake it, how often will you win? ... A hundred percent." (Ep. 137)
- "It's sort of like what I call the slot machine that you don't have to put money into." (Ep. 137)

**Full Teaching Sequence (Ep. 137):**
1. Identify the stable situation with "locked" variables
2. Shake the box -- introduce unpredictability, change frames, flip-flop
3. Observers call this "chaos" or "incompetence"
4. Check the new configuration
5. If favorable, stop. If not, shake again
6. Since no one else can shake, your win is inevitable given enough shakes

**Applied to (Ep. 115, immigration):**
- Adding variables (combine immigration with jobs, prison reform, black community issues)
- Shake until you find a configuration where everyone can claim a win
- Make results hard to measure so all sides can claim victory

**Episodes:** 76, 115, 137

### 8. Moist Robot Hypothesis

**Core mechanism:** Humans are programmable biological machines ("moist robots"). We respond to inputs in predictable ways. Free will is an illusion -- not because outcomes are predetermined, but because our responses are mechanical given our inputs. This has profound implications: persuasion works because we ARE programmable, and nobody is immune.

**Key Quotes:**
- "I think there's just a little moist computer happening in there, and it does what it has to do given the inputs." (Ep. 51, with Naval Ravikant)
- "Nobody's immune from persuasion. Sometimes people say, 'Well, if you learn about persuasion, then once you know how it works, then you're immune, right?' Nope, doesn't work that way. All the awareness in the world doesn't protect you from good persuasion." (Ep. 76)
- "How easily hacked is the human brain?" (Candace Owens, quoted approvingly in Ep. 137)

**Connection to simulation theory (Ep. 137):**
Adams extends the moist robot concept through the simulation hypothesis -- if we're programs in a simulation, free will doesn't exist, and our "history" may be generated on demand rather than having actually occurred.

**Practical implication:** Since nobody is immune to persuasion, even intelligence agency professionals can be "brainwashed" by cognitive scientists working on political campaigns. (Ep. 76)

**Episodes:** 51, 76, 137

### 9. Mental Prisons

**Core mechanism:** People trap themselves in belief systems that prevent independent thought. The prison of the past (letting history dictate present choices), the prison of party loyalty (surrendering leverage by guaranteeing your vote), and the prison of confirmation bias (inability to see disconfirming evidence).

**Key Quotes:**
- "Don't be a captive of the past. Don't let history be your prison." (Ep. 28)
- "In order to mock the idea that you don't want to be in a mental prison, the only way you can mock it is to put yourself in the mental prison." (Ep. 28)
- "Most of our problems now are prisons of the past. North Korea -- it's not a current problem, it's a prison of the past problem. Russia, same problem. Iran, same problem." (Ep. 28)
- "The young people in the Democratic Party are trapped in a mental cage, a mental prison." (Ep. 133)

**The Trap Design (Ep. 28):**
Adams deliberately frames the "escape your mental prison" message so that critics can only attack it by demonstrating they are in the prison. Any attack based on historical grievance proves the point.

**Episodes:** 28, 129, 132, 133

### 10. The Thinking Class vs. Non-Thinking Class

**Core mechanism:** Most people have never been trained to think -- specifically, to compare alternatives. They evaluate single options ("this is bad, stop it") rather than comparing two bad options to find the less-bad one. This is not an intelligence issue; it's a training issue.

**Key Quotes:**
- "People believe that they know how to think and make decisions, but in fact, most people don't. I say this without any unkindness. People who have not learned how to think don't know how. You actually have to go to school to learn it in most cases." (Ep. 111)
- "Here is how most people think: 'Hey, that thing is bad. We have to stop doing that.' That's it." (Ep. 111)
- "If you're not comparing things, you're not thinking." (Ep. 111)
- "If you can't deal with the actual situation, just leave the conversation." (Ep. 109)

**What training teaches (Ep. 111):**
- Compare alternatives, not just evaluate one option
- Consider short-term vs. long-term tradeoffs
- Recognize that "the ends justify the means" is not an argument -- it describes every rational decision ever made
- Understand that emotional activation (children in distress) disables critical thinking

**The Catholic Priest Example (Ep. 111):**
A priest on CNN was asked about the long-term argument for border policy and responded, "That's the old argument that the ends justify the means." Adams: "That is almost the opposite of thinking because, you know what other situations the ends justify the means? All of the situations that we choose to do."

**Episodes:** 109, 111, 118

### 11. Sell Past the Close

**Core mechanism:** In persuasion, once you've won the argument, stop talking. Anything you say after the sale is closed might undo the sale. The most common self-inflicted wound in public discourse.

**Key Quotes:**
- "Once you're done selling, stop talking, because anything you say after the sale is closed might undo the sale." (Ep. 121)
- "All he had to do is stop talking. Just say, 'Thank you very much, thanks for having me, I'm out of here.' But he didn't; he kept talking." (Ep. 121, about Steve Cortes using "thuggery")

**Case Study (Ep. 121):**
Steve Cortes on CNN maneuvered his opponent (Angela Rye) into attacking Chuck Schumer as a racist -- a total win for Cortes's side. Then he kept talking and used the word "thuggery," which has racial overtones, instantly undoing his entire victory.

**Episodes:** 121

### 12. The Brainwashing Defense

**Core mechanism:** Adams raises the question of whether people who were persuaded by professional cognitive scientists to believe false things (e.g., that Trump was literally Hitler) could use brainwashing as a legal or moral defense for their subsequent actions. He concludes it probably can never be a legal defense because everyone would use it, but it might actually be true.

**Key Quotes:**
- "You will have in evidence professional cognitive experts, people who know how to brainwash. It's their science; it's their specialty. You can see that they're the experts in brainwashing. You can see the result of their work... And then you could see the result: you can see that the people were in fact brainwashed." (Ep. 76)
- "Imagine how you'd feel if you'd been brainwashed into overthrowing your own country and none of it was real." (Ep. 111)
- "The President's problem with the media is not that they're just fake news; the problem is because they're fake news, it has brainwashed half the country into thinking that a monster is running the country." (Ep. 121)

**The Impossible Mental Journey (Ep. 111):**
Adams maps the cognitive distance the anti-Trump side would have to travel: from "Trump and Russia colluded" past "it didn't happen" all the way to "the FBI was behind it." He argues: "No brain can go that far. It is as close as you can get to an impossible mental journey."

### 13. Visual Persuasion Beats Conceptual Arguments

**Core mechanism:** Pictures, sounds, and visceral imagery always defeat abstract concepts in the persuasion arena. "Concepts lose to pictures and sound every time."

**Key Quotes:**
- "Imagine any of this conversation without the word 'cage' or the photos that showed people in cages." (Ep. 109)
- "You can't beat the visual of children in distress. You can't beat the sound of them crying. Those are gold-plated nuclear visual emotional images." (Ep. 111)
- "Their counter-attack to these visuals and the audio, the visceral stuff, is concepts. A concept like, 'Well, if we don't do this, things might be worse in the future.' ... Concepts lose to pictures and sound every time." (Ep. 111)

**Application:** The administration's failure was having only concepts (long-term thinking, deterrence theory) to counter the opposition's visuals (crying children, cage photos). Adams suggests they needed counter-visuals: children video-calling parents, graphs of short-term vs. long-term outcomes.

**Episodes:** 109, 111

### 14. The Fake "Because" / Escape Hatch

**Core mechanism:** People trapped in cognitive dissonance need a face-saving reason to change their mind. The reason doesn't have to be logically rigorous -- it just needs to be good enough to let them exit without feeling like they were complete fools. Adams calls this a "fake because" or "mental escape hatch."

**Key Quotes:**
- "They need a fake 'because.' They need something to look at where they can say, 'All right, I didn't like this, but I'm gonna put up with it.' A mental escape hatch." (Ep. 121)
- "For the people with Trump Derangement Syndrome, there are two forces happening. One is their movie keeps getting worse and worse while the country keeps getting better and better." (Ep. 121)

**Connection to hitting bottom:** Like an alcoholic, the person in cognitive dissonance must hit bottom before they can change. The escape hatch is what enables the turn. (Ep. 121)

**Episodes:** 121, 132

### 16. Emotional Override of Critical Thinking

**Core mechanism:** Certain stimuli -- especially children in distress -- completely disable the brain's capacity for rational analysis. This is biological, not a character flaw. It affects experts (doctors, healthcare professionals) just as much as laypeople.

**Key Quotes:**
- "Any adult who hears a child crying in distress -- that goes right to our most basic instincts, and we go 'Whoop!' Reason doesn't matter." (Ep. 111)
- "If you make a decision based on hearing the screams, you're not part of the thinking class." (Ep. 111)
- "The medical community, who are just as susceptible to the emotional override of their critical thinking. Just because people are also trained in sciences -- they're healthcare professionals -- that doesn't make them rational all the time." (Ep. 111)

**Teaching point:** Adams distinguishes between feeling the emotion (appropriate, human) and making decisions based on the emotion (dangerous, irrational). He repeatedly leads with empathy before making analytical points, modeling the technique he recommends.

**Episodes:** 109, 111

### 17. Multiple Explanation Test

**Core mechanism:** When a phenomenon generates many different explanations and none converges, the real cause is probably cognitive dissonance, not any of the proposed explanations. Each person is constructing their own "movie" to explain confusion they cannot resolve.

**Key Quotes:**
- "I've taught you that when you have that many different explanations for a thing, probably none of them are true. It's more likely that there's cognitive dissonance and that everybody is inventing their own explanation for it." (Ep. 76, on the Middle East)
- "When you see lots of different explanations for why something unusual happened, it probably means nobody knows." (Ep. 124, on Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez's primary win)

**Applied to Middle East (Ep. 76):**
Adams argues the trigger for Middle East cognitive dissonance is 70 years of God apparently favoring Israel despite Palestinian prayers. The unresolvable contradiction generates dozens of incompatible explanatory narratives. The solution is to address the cognitive dissonance trigger, not the individual narratives.

**Episodes:** 76, 124

### 18. Authoring Others

**Core mechanism:** You do not merely react to other people; you create them. By defining the relationship frame (competitor vs. enemy, friend vs. adversary), you shape how the other person behaves toward you. This extends the moist robot hypothesis to interpersonal and international relations.

**Key Quotes:**
- "He isn't reacting to Putin. He isn't reacting to Kim Jong Un. He isn't reacting to President Xi. He is causing them. He's creating them. He is authoring them." (Ep. 137)
- "When I would smoke the marijuana back in my college days, it would feel to me like other people were being nicer to me... I realized I was changing the people around me." (Ep. 137)
- "People's attitudes, their opinions, the way they feel, doesn't come entirely from them. Some of it does... but how they feel when they're in the room with just you and them has mostly to do, in that moment, with you." (Ep. 137)

## Teaching Sequences Found

### How to Compare (Ep. 111)
Adams's most sustained pedagogical sequence on thinking:
1. Untrained thinking: "That thing is bad. Stop doing it."
2. Trained thinking: "That thing is bad. Compared to what?"
3. Example: Taxes are bad (untrained) vs. Taxes are bad but what happens to society without them? (trained)
4. Apply to immigration: Children in cages is bad (untrained) vs. Children in cages is bad but the alternative creates more suffering long-term (trained)
5. The Catholic priest's "ends justify the means" critique is the opposite of thinking -- it rejects all cost-benefit analysis

### How to Diagnose Cognitive Dissonance (Ep. 76, 117)
1. Look for word salad -- sentences that parse grammatically but convey no meaning
2. Look for multiple contradictory explanations for the same event
3. Look for emotional escalation without factual claims
4. Test: say something that contradicts their worldview and watch for the "freeze"
5. Self-check: acknowledge your own confirmation bias openly

### How to Handle an Angry Mob (Ep. 121)
1. Ask "Which one of you is in charge?"
2. This forces the group to process an organizational question
3. If someone claims leadership, ask "What specifically is bothering you today?"
4. Find common ground: "I'm on your side"
5. Reframe: present the two bad choices so they realize you share a goal

### How to Break Someone's Bubble (Ep. 121, 132)
1. Accept that facts, logic, and good arguments will not work
2. The person needs a "fake because" -- a face-saving exit
3. Do something so dramatically inconsistent with their movie that the movie breaks
4. The action must violate their narrative so completely it cannot be explained within their existing framework
5. Example: pardoning a political enemy, doing something unambiguously good for a group they believe you hate

## Case Studies

### 1. Border Children / Immigration Crisis (Eps. 109-116, 121, 128)
Adams applies nearly every cognitive framework to this single issue:
- **Analogies destroy thinking**: Holocaust comparisons, CPS comparisons, kindergarten comparisons -- all invalid
- **Word-thinking**: "cage" vs. "facility" changes nothing about the actual situation
- **Emotional override**: Children crying disables all critical faculties
- **Short-term vs. long-term**: The untrained thinker sees only the immediate horror; the trained thinker compares two bad options
- **Mass hysteria**: Media amplification converts a real problem into a disproportionate political weapon
- **Visual beats conceptual**: Photos of cages destroy any abstract argument about long-term incentives
- **Cognitive dissonance prediction**: Adams predicted the anti-Trump side would "go crazy" when North Korea progress falsified their worldview; immigration became the outlet

### 2. Spygate (Ep. 76)
- Four-point logical contradiction demonstrates mass cognitive dissonance
- Spy vs. informant distinction is word salad
- Intelligence community was "brainwashed" by the same persuasion that affected the general public
- Nobody is immune to persuasion, not even experts

### 3. "Animals" / MS-13 (Ep. 78)
- Adams identifies this as the first time Trump critics "saw behind the curtain" -- could see both movies simultaneously
- The out-of-context misrepresentation was so obvious that even hostile observers had to acknowledge the media manipulation
- First crack in the confirmation bias cascade

### 4. Kanye West / Dragon Energy (Ep. 32, 28)
- Kanye's tweet demonstrated "visual persuasion" ("dragon energy" creates an image)
- Breaking the "couch lock" of guaranteed Democratic votes
- Adams frames it as unlocking leverage: once a voting bloc is willing to switch, it gains all the power
- The "mental prison" concept -- critics can only attack free thinking by demonstrating they're trapped

### 5. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez Primary Win (Ep. 124)
- Multiple explanation test: when people offer many different reasons for her win (gender, ethnicity, low turnout, luck), "it probably means nobody knows" -- cognitive dissonance

### 6. Cognitive Dissonance in the Middle East (Ep. 76)
- Trigger: 70 years of God apparently favoring Israel despite prayers
- Generates dozens of incompatible explanatory narratives
- Solution requires addressing the dissonance trigger, not the individual narratives
- Expert knowledge of the region is irrelevant because the problem is psychological, not factual

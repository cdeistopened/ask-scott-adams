## Frameworks Found

### 1. The Persuasion Filter

**Core claim:** Most political and media events are better understood through a persuasion lens than a fact-debate lens. The "persuasion filter" is Adams's master framework — all other frameworks nest inside it.

**Key teaching:** "In the realm of politics, facts don't matter... The world of politics was always 100% persuasion. Always. It was just different. The thing that Trump has added is that he just does more of it and he does it better because he understands the field of persuasion and he puts a high priority on it." (ep 104)

**Extended claim:** Persuasion works even when the target knows the technique being used. "You can tell people what you're doing right in front of them and say, 'Look, I'm going to make you look at this thing first because it will influence how you think about this thing,' and the person will say, 'Yeah, I'm not so easily manipulated,' and then you can do it right in front of them and it will influence how they think about the other thing." (ep 104)

**Prediction test:** The best filter is the one that makes the best predictions. Adams repeatedly tests the persuasion filter against the "facts matter" filter and the "Trump is lucky" filter by tracking whose predictions come true. (ep 65, 81, 89)

**Episodes:** 34, 36, 50, 52, 61, 65, 68, 69, 70, 73, 81, 86, 89, 104, 106, 107

### 2. Two Movies on One Screen

**Core mechanism:** Same set of facts, two completely opposite interpretations running simultaneously. Neither side can see the other's movie. This isn't disagreement about values — it's disagreement about what is literally happening.

**Key teaching:** "Once you realize that people are literally observing and interpreting their world in completely different ways, then you're free to start realizing that your ego is not who you are. It's just a tool that you can use." (ep 106)

**Amplification by complexity:** "When you have complicated situations, people can just craft that complicated situation into whatever they want to see." A 500-page report guarantees everyone sees what they want. (ep 105)

**Yanni/Laurel proof:** The audio illusion where different people literally hear different words from the same sound wave became Adams's go-to physical demonstration that "you hear what you expect to hear" applies to all perception. (ep 68, 69, 70, 79)

**Episodes:** 34, 36, 61, 68, 69, 70, 73, 79, 86, 105, 106, 107

### 3. Visual Persuasion vs. Conceptual Arguments

**Core claim:** Visuals always beat concepts. A picture beats a thousand words. A movie beats a thousand pictures. This is the single strongest persuasion tool after fear.

**Key teaching:** Adams on General Hayden's Auschwitz tweet — "By adding the visual, the analogy is just sort of a carrier. The analogy isn't the important part; the important part was the visual. You put that visual in your head and then you have this automatic association. What the good General is doing is top-rate CIA-quality persuasion disguised as a bad analogy because the analogy is ridiculous, but the picture is gold." (ep 107)

**Singapore Summit video analysis:** Trump's video for Kim was "chock-full with the strongest, most well-executed persuasion that you've probably ever seen in one place" — visual, thinking past the sale, contrast principle, ego as tool, "what if" framing. Critics missed it because they don't recognize technique. (ep 101, 103, 104)

**Anti-pattern — analogies:** "Is there anything analogies can do except make things worse? Whenever anybody uses an analogy in a persuasive context, it just totally blows up." Analogies invite the audience to pick apart the comparison instead of accepting the point. Visuals bypass this entirely. (ep 29, 33, 43, 70, 90, 107)

**Episodes:** 40, 41, 47, 82, 91, 101, 103, 104, 107

### 4. Pacing and Leading

**Core mechanism:** Match someone's reality before redirecting it. You must first demonstrate that you understand and share their experience before you can move them to a new position.

**Key teaching on border debate:** "First, pace them. Children being separated from their parents and put in prison is the worst. It's the worst. First, agree, because you do agree... then say, 'So what should we do?' And then walk them through the options." (ep 106)

**Diplomatic application:** Trump praising Kim Jong-un is pacing — making Kim feel comfortable and understood before leading him toward denuclearization. "Praising Kim is one of the ways that Kim will feel comfortable with the president. It's one of the ways that Kim will learn to trust him." (ep 103)

**Trust as mechanism:** "What is the very best way to receive trust? Give trust... Psychologically speaking, the very best way to get trust from someone who is reluctant to give it is to give it to them first." (ep 103)

**Seven-word mastery example:** "How can we stay safe today, officer?" — layers pacing (we're on the same team), leading (here's what I need), flattery (you're the authority), and fear reduction in a single sentence. (ep 82)

**Episodes:** 33, 47, 61, 82, 90, 92, 98, 103, 106

### 5. Kill Shot / Linguistic Kill Shot / Branding

**Core mechanism:** A memorable label that permanently reframes a target. Once the label sticks, everything the target does is filtered through it. The best kill shots are visual, unique, and confirmed by the target's own behavior.

**Key examples analyzed:**
- **"Punchy De Niro"** — "That is the stickiest nickname you've ever heard in your life." Sticky because it's visual (getting punched), confirmed by behavior (De Niro's angry outbursts), and raises a real question (brain injury from boxing scenes → anger issues). (ep 102, 103)
- **"Spygate"** — Forces the enemy to use your word. Every time media says "Spygate," they're reinforcing Trump's frame. (ep 77)
- **"Crooked Hillary," "Low Energy Jeb," "Little Marco"** — Each one visual, sticky, confirmed by observed behavior. (ep 47, 50, 65, 87)

**Kill shot design principles:**
1. Must be visual (you can picture it)
2. Must be unique to the target (not generic insult)
3. Must be confirmed by the target's own behavior (the label "sticks" because evidence keeps appearing)
4. Bonus: raises a question the audience can't stop thinking about

**Counter-strategy:** Almost impossible to defend against. The target can't deny it without reinforcing it. (ep 77, 87)

**Episodes:** 47, 50, 65, 77, 87, 102, 103

### 6. Thinking Past the Sale

**Core mechanism:** Get the audience to accept the premise by debating the details of an already-decided outcome. Skip the "whether" and go straight to "how."

**Key teaching:** The Singapore Summit video "makes you think 'past the sale,' past peace to how good peace will be." Instead of debating whether peace is possible, viewers imagine what their beachfront condos will look like. (ep 103)

**Diplomatic example:** "We've decided North Korea will end up without nukes" — framing denuclearization as already settled. The only question is the timeline and process. (ep 75, 80)

**Episodes:** 41, 50, 75, 80, 82, 101, 103

### 7. Ego as Tool vs. Ego as Identity

**Core mechanism:** Master persuaders use ego as a dial they can turn up or down. Losers treat ego as their identity and can't adjust it. This is one of Adams's most developed frameworks.

**Full teaching sequence:**
1. **Ramp UP for campaigning:** Big ego projects confidence, dominance, certainty. Essential for winning attention and votes. (ep 60, 80)
2. **Ramp DOWN for diplomacy:** Once you've won, lower the ego to build trust and collaboration. Trump shifted from campaign-mode aggression to diplomatic warmth with Kim. (ep 80, 82, 101)
3. **Ego prevents value creation:** "If your mindset is that slavery is where your group identity is largely influenced, could you say, 'Yeah, I think my strategy in life is to give things away for free'?... The most effective common strategy that white people use to succeed is figuring out how to give somebody else something for nothing because that's how they get something." Group ego can prevent the reciprocity strategy from working. (ep 104)
4. **Ego as obstacle to learning:** "Once you understand that you could be in the wrong movie, or your movie might not be the one that predicts the best, then you can say, 'Oh, my ego is not helping me out here.'" (ep 106)

**Cialdini connection:** "You become what you say" — commitment/consistency principle means public ego positions lock you in. (ep 82, 100)

**Episodes:** 60, 71, 80, 82, 100, 101, 104, 106

### 8. Cognitive Dissonance Detection System

**Core mechanism:** Adams developed a taxonomy of "tells" — verbal and behavioral patterns that indicate someone is experiencing cognitive dissonance rather than reasoning clearly. This is his most systematic framework.

**The Tells (full taxonomy):**

| Tell | Description | Episodes |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Collapse to one variable** | Reducing a complex multi-variable situation to one factor | 81, 106 |
| **Expand to absurd absolute** | "He's LITERALLY Hitler" — taking a real concern to an impossible extreme | 69, 81 |
| **Mind-reader hallucination** | Claiming to know someone's interior thoughts without evidence | 81, 105 |
| **Word salad** | Response that sounds like language but contains no actual argument | 71, 81, 83 |
| **"So" tell** | Starting a rebuttal with "So what you're saying is..." followed by a strawman | 69, 71, 81 |
| **"Bro"/"Dude" tell** | Dismissive address substituting for argument | 81 |
| **"Ahahahaha" (reason-free mockery)** | Laughter as substitute for counterargument | 81 |
| **Laundry list tell** | Listing 5+ weak complaints because no single one is strong enough | 100, 106 |
| **Ignoring prediction track record** | Continuing to trust a filter that has failed repeatedly | 81, 89 |
| **Unspecific doom forecast** | "Something bad will happen eventually" without mechanism | 81, 107 |
| **Oddly specific denial** | Denying something very specific that nobody asked about — tell for guilt | 74 |
| **Trigger identification** | Recognizing what specific stimulus caused the dissonance | 90 |

**Laundry list analysis (extended):** "The anti-Trumpers who will list quite often five things... The first thing I should say is I'm not relying on science for this. As far as I know, there's no scientific study that would support what I'm going to tell you... My thinking is that the people who have decided they dislike the President started with big reasons in the beginning... It's now been 520 days of the presidency, and he has made nuclear war far less likely... So all of the big stuff that people had in their heads as 'these are the reasons we hate Trump'—the big, big things—they just don't work anymore." When pressed, they stack weak complaints: "con man" + "liar" + "cult of personality" + "Russia" + "children in cages" = "BAM, Hitler!" (ep 106)

**Episodes:** 69, 71, 74, 81, 83, 89, 90, 100, 106

### 9. Fear as the Strongest Persuasion Tool

**Core claim:** Fear is the most powerful persuasion tool, bar none. It overrides all other considerations.

**Key teaching:** When fear is deployed, facts become irrelevant. The "children in cages" narrative works despite factual inaccuracies because it triggers parental fear — the most primal fear humans have. (ep 106)

**Contrast principle extension:** Fear is most effective when paired with hope — draw two pictures, one terrible (what happens if you don't act) and one wonderful (what happens if you do). The Singapore Summit video used this: nuclear destruction vs. beachfront prosperity. (ep 40, 54, 57, 80, 90, 101)

**Episodes:** 38, 40, 106, 107

### 10. The High-Ground Maneuver

**Core mechanism:** When stuck in a losing argument, rise above it to a level where no one can disagree. Instead of arguing the specifics, reframe at a higher level of abstraction where you occupy unassailable moral ground.

**Key teaching:** "Be strategic" beats "stop being a victim." Positive framing of the same advice is the high-ground version. (ep 74, 75)

**Diplomatic application:** "It's better to be friends" is the ultimate high-ground position in international relations — nobody can argue against friendship as a concept. (ep 90, 98)

**Episodes:** 38, 75, 82, 90, 98

### 11. The Halfpinion

**Core mechanism:** A "halfpinion" is a complaint without an alternative. A full opinion compares options: "I prefer X over Y." A halfpinion just states "X is bad" without proposing Y. Adams argues halfpinions are not real opinions and are a tell for weak thinking.

**Key teaching:** "A regular opinion is when you say, 'I prefer this situation over this situation.' A full opinion is, 'I've looked at all the alternatives. We could go this way, we could go this way; I prefer this one over this one.' That's an opinion... A halfpinion is when you say half of that." (ep 107)

**Extended — the missing "and":** "When you look at the complaints from the anti-Trumpers, they're all missing the 'and.' A typical anti-Trumper will say, 'The President has lied 3,600 times since he came into office.' And? 'And that killed people and hurt the economy.' They always leave out the 'and.'" (ep 107)

**Episodes:** 107

### 12. Analogies Don't Persuade (Critical Anti-Pattern)

**Core claim:** Analogies are the worst persuasion tool. They feel persuasive to the person using them but always backfire because the audience picks apart the comparison instead of accepting the point.

**Key teaching:** "Is there anything analogies can do except make things worse? Whenever anybody uses an analogy in a persuasive context, it just totally blows up. It doesn't do anything good." (ep 107)

**Why they fail:** The audience's brain automatically searches for differences between the analogy and the real situation. Finding differences feels like "winning" the argument, even if the underlying point was valid. (ep 29, 33, 43)

**Magical thinking triad:** Pattern recognition + analogies + "history repeats itself" = all forms of magical thinking that feel rational but aren't. (ep 93)

**Episodes:** 29, 33, 43, 70, 90, 93, 107

### 13. Adding Variables / Expanding the Scope / Shake the Box

**Core mechanism:** When a negotiation or argument is stuck, add new variables to create more possible solutions. A 2-variable problem has limited solutions; a 10-variable problem has exponentially more.

**Key teaching:** Instead of a binary "nukes or no nukes" negotiation, Trump expanded to include economic development, security guarantees, personal relationships, international prestige, tourism, real estate development. Each new variable creates new trade possibilities. (ep 41, 43, 55, 75, 80, 98)

**Limits:** "Shaking the box" works for ideas but not for physical infrastructure. Dividing California into three states is physical box-shaking with too much friction. Trump shakes ideas, not infrastructure. (ep 105)

**Episodes:** 41, 43, 55, 75, 80, 98, 105

### 14. Wanting vs. Deciding

**Core mechanism:** There is a fundamental difference between wanting something and deciding to have it. A decision means you will pay whatever cost is necessary. Wanting means you'll try until it gets hard.

**Key teaching:** "We've decided North Korea will end up without nukes." This isn't a prediction — it's a frame that changes the entire negotiation. When you've "decided," the only question is how, not whether. (ep 73, 75, 80)

**Episodes:** 73, 75, 80

### 15. Mental Prisons

**Core mechanism:** Beliefs that limit what you think is possible. The prison isn't factual constraints — it's psychological ones. People don't attempt things because they've been told (or told themselves) those things are impossible.

**Key teaching:** Kanye West's message was powerful because he was breaking a specific mental prison: that Black voters must vote Democrat. "When your vote is locked in, it doesn't have influence. Because if you're not at least threatening to go to the other side, you have no power." (ep 44)

**Connection to ego:** Mental prisons are maintained by ego. If your identity is wrapped around a belief, you can't examine it without threatening your sense of self. (ep 26, 29, 33, 38, 43)

**Episodes:** 26, 29, 33, 38, 43, 44, 82, 101

### 16. Talent Stack

**Core mechanism:** Combining mediocre skills into a rare and valuable combination. You don't need to be world-class at anything — you need to be decent at several complementary things.

**Key teaching:** Adams is not the best artist, not the funniest comedian, not the best business writer — but the combination of those three skills made Dilbert uniquely valuable. (ep 27, 29, 61)

**Application to persuasion:** Trump's talent stack — branding + negotiation + entertainment + social media + shamelessness + work ethic — creates a combination no traditional politician can match. (ep 61)

**Episodes:** 27, 29, 61, 104

### 17. Facts Don't Matter (in Persuasion Context)

**Core claim:** In the persuasion context, facts have zero persuasive power. Facts only persuade people who were already persuaded. The "children in cages" narrative works despite being factually inaccurate because "facts don't matter to persuasion. It just doesn't matter." (ep 106)

**Key distinction:** Facts matter to outcomes (walk in front of a truck, the truck kills you). Facts don't matter to how people make decisions. "The way people make decisions is so divorced from facts and always has been." (ep 104)

**Where facts work:** Engineering, science, product development, personal life. Where they don't: politics, any situation where identity and emotion are primary. (ep 50, 52, 104)

**The expert's dilemma (Lakoff):** George Lakoff understands persuasion technique, sees Trump using it skillfully, but can't convince his own side because "he still thinks facts matter. He knows they don't matter to persuasion because he observed it... But he can't leave the 'facts matter' field." (ep 106)

**Episodes:** 50, 52, 104, 106

### 18. Reciprocity as Persuasion Strategy

**Core mechanism:** Give something of value first, expecting nothing in return. This creates obligation and trust simultaneously.

**Key teaching — the salt salesman story:** Adams's neighbor became wealthy selling salt — the most generic product imaginable — by showing up at a grocery store on a Saturday and helping reorganize shelves for free. "He didn't sell salt; he sold himself. He sold greater value than a regular salt seller can provide. He sold credibility. He used the power of reciprocity to give this guy something." (ep 104)

**Identity barrier to reciprocity:** If your group identity involves a history of exploitation, the reciprocity strategy ("give something for nothing to people with power") feels psychologically impossible. This is Adams's most controversial analysis — that the legacy of slavery creates a specific barrier to the most effective common success strategy. (ep 104)

**Episodes:** 104

### 19. The Progression of Awareness

**Core mechanism:** Four phases of political/persuasion awareness, from least to most sophisticated:

1. **"The other side is evil"** — Default. You disagree because they're bad people.
2. **"The other side has different information"** — Slightly better. You recognize an information gap.
3. **"Both sides are seeing different movies"** — Two Movies framework. You understand that perception itself differs.
4. **"I can spot my own triggers"** — Master level. You recognize when your own cognitive dissonance is being triggered and can compensate.

**Key teaching:** Most people never get past Phase 1. The persuasion-aware person operates at Phase 4. (ep 90)

**Episodes:** 90

### 20. Vagueness as Persuasion Tool

**Core mechanism:** Intentional vagueness lets the audience fill in their own details, making the message feel personally relevant to everyone simultaneously.

**Key teaching:** Trump's campaign promises were deliberately vague ("Make America Great Again") so each supporter could imagine their own version of greatness. Specificity would have narrowed the coalition. (ep 50)

**Episodes:** 50

### 21. Discrediting Sources in Advance (Pre-suasion)

**Core mechanism:** Before the opposing information arrives, plant doubt about the source. This way, when people encounter the information, they've already been primed to dismiss it.

**Key teaching:** Trump's "fake news" labeling functions as pre-suasion — by the time a critical story runs, supporters have already been inoculated against the source. (ep 77)

**Episodes:** 77

### 22. Flattery as Self-Persuasion

**Core mechanism:** When you flatter someone, you partially convince yourself of what you're saying. Countries flattering Trump end up convincing themselves he's someone worth working with.

**Key teaching:** The flattery loop — Kim flatters Trump, Trump flatters Kim, and in the process both leaders talk themselves into the relationship being real. (ep 65, 103)

### 23. The 2-of-6 Rule of Humor

**Core mechanism:** For something to be funny, it needs at least 2 of 6 elements: Mean, Bizarre, Clever, Naughty, Recognizable, Cute.

**Key teaching:** This is a diagnostic tool for analyzing why things are or aren't funny. "Punchy De Niro" scores on Mean + Recognizable + Clever. (ep 63, 102)

### 24. Punching Up vs. Punching Down

**Core mechanism:** Comedy directed at someone more powerful (punching up) is funnier and more socially acceptable than comedy directed at someone less powerful (punching down). The power differential determines the audience's emotional response.

### 25. Odd Number Preference

### 26. Occam's Razor Doesn't Work in Human Affairs

**Core mechanism:** "The simplest explanation" is whatever you already believe. Both sides always think their explanation is the simple one. In human affairs, Occam's Razor is useless because simplicity is subjective.

**Key teaching:** "How did the universe come into being? What's the simplest explanation? One word: evolution. One word: evolution... How about you? What do you think? God? Okay, turns out there are two simple explanations." (ep 105)

**Episodes:** 105

### 30. Respect vs. Racism Frame

**Core mechanism:** Reframe the unsolvable problem "racism" into the achievable goal "respect." You can't eliminate racism (interior belief), but you can build systems of mutual respect (observable behavior).

**Key teaching:** "Respect" is actionable, measurable, and doesn't require mind-reading. "Racism" requires knowing someone's interior state, which is impossible. Focus on what you can observe and change. (ep 92)

### 32. Placebo Effect Extended / Simulation Steering

**Key teaching:** "Fatalism + optimism" — accept what you can't change, be aggressive about what you can. The combination prevents both helplessness and delusion. (ep 94, 97)

### 35. Removing the Reason

**Core mechanism:** The most powerful negotiation move is not to win concessions but to eliminate the other side's reason for opposing you. Once the reason is gone, everything else follows naturally.

**Key teaching:** "He took away North Korea's reason. He took away the reason. The reason that they had nuclear weapons was to do things like get world respect. Done. They have it... They had nuclear weapons to protect themselves from the United States. Does that matter anymore? No. That reason was taken away." (ep 108)

**Why this is missed:** Pundits focus on concrete concessions (inspection rights, missile tests) while the strategic move — eliminating the reason for the conflict — is invisible to traditional analysis. (ep 108)

**Episodes:** 108

### 36. Audience of One

**Core mechanism:** Persuasion must be designed for the actual target, not for observers. Critics of Trump's Singapore video missed that "President Trump did not make a video to show to The New Yorker magazine... He made a video for one guy." The style matched North Korean propaganda aesthetics because that's what the audience responds to.

**Key teaching:** "Who else said, 'Of course it looks weird to us, it's not for us'? Do you ever watch a children's cartoon on TV and you say to yourself, 'This children's cartoon does not appeal to me'? Do you know why? It wasn't made for you." (ep 103)

### 37. Priorities Framework (Truth vs. Winning)

**Core mechanism:** Two hypothetical leaders — one who prioritizes truth, one who prioritizes winning (defined as best outcomes for citizens). Adams argues the truth-prioritizer will always lose because in politics there is no agreed-upon truth, only competing narratives.

**Key teaching:** "Imagine a second hypothetical leader, someone else who comes in and says, 'There's been too much lying in this Trump administration. I will only tell you the truth.'... Which leader do you want? Because the person who only tells you the truth has prioritized the truth over a good result." (ep 104)

**The Sam Harris critique:** Harris prioritizes truth/accuracy. Adams acknowledges this works for science, engineering, personal life — "most realms." But argues it's catastrophic in politics where "there is no truth. In science, sometimes there is. In math, quite often there is... In politics, that's not a thing." (ep 104)

**Episodes:** 104

### 38. Dog Whistle Filter vs. Hammer/Nail Filter

**Core mechanism:** Two competing explanations for perceived racism in political speech. The "dog whistle" filter says politicians embed hidden racist messages. The "hammer/nail" filter says people who look for racism find it everywhere, like someone with a hammer seeing every problem as a nail.

**Test:** Whichever filter makes better predictions about future behavior is the better filter. Adams argues the hammer/nail filter has better predictive power. (ep 89)

**Episodes:** 89

### 39. Whack-a-Mole Persuasion / "Fits a Pattern" Fallacy

**Core mechanism:** Debunking individual false claims fails against "fits a pattern" arguments because the pattern claim is unfalsifiable. You knock down one claim, another pops up. The pattern believer says "see, there are so many examples" even as each individual example is debunked.

**Episodes:** 71

### 40. Reversible Giveaways as Trust-Building

"Everything I listed can be taken away easily... Both sides have done absolutely nothing they can't be taken back in a moment. Now, I'm not saying that's bad. My next point is that it's good." (ep 102)

### 41. Personal Identity vs. Group Identity

**Core mechanism:** Everyone has two identities: individual (what you've done) and tribal (what your group has done). Group identity influences decisions even when the individual has no personal connection to the group's history.

**Key teaching:** White supremacists claiming credit for Edison's achievements is the same cognitive error as anyone claiming group identity as personal achievement. "How do you get credit—how does the white supremacist living in a shack somewhere get credit for the fact that Edison was smart?" But the mechanism is real — tribal identity shapes behavior regardless of its logical validity. (ep 104)

**Episodes:** 104

### 42. "Willing Things Into Existence"

**Core mechanism:** Public statements of trust/confidence don't predict reality — they create it. When Trump says "I trust Kim," he's not describing a feeling; he's constructing a reality that both parties can inhabit.

**Key teaching:** "He's creating that world. He's not predicting it; he's creating it by just defining it as true and then living in it and letting Kim live in it as well." (ep 103)

**Episodes:** 73, 75, 80, 103

### 43. Children vs. Adult Frames

**Core mechanism:** Child thinking focuses on one variable ("I don't like school"). Adult thinking holds multiple variables simultaneously ("school is unpleasant but produces long-term benefit"). Adams uses this to categorize political arguments.

**Key teaching:** "When you see people arguing about the children being ripped from their parents... The Trump supporters are saying, 'There's a bigger picture.' Taking children from parents is terrible; we all agree on that part. But what exactly is the alternative?" (ep 106)

## Teaching Sequences

### Sequence 1: The Complete Persuasion Stack (How Trump Persuades)

Adams builds this across dozens of episodes. The layers, from foundation to apex:

1. **Fear or hope as foundation** — Establish emotional stakes (ep 38, 40)
2. **Visual persuasion** — Create mental images, not arguments (ep 40, 41, 47, 82)
3. **Thinking past the sale** — Skip "whether" to "how" (ep 41, 50, 75)
4. **Pacing and leading** — Match reality, then redirect (ep 33, 47, 61, 82)
5. **Kill shot / branding** — Sticky labels that reframe permanently (ep 47, 50, 65, 77)
6. **Contrast principle** — Two futures: terrible vs. wonderful (ep 40, 54, 57, 80)
7. **Ego management** — Ramp up for fighting, down for diplomacy (ep 60, 80, 82)
8. **Vagueness** — Let audience fill in their own details (ep 50)
9. **Repetition** — Same simple message, hundreds of times (ep 50)

### Sequence 2: How to Detect and Counter Cognitive Dissonance

Adams's diagnostic system taught across episodes 69-106:

1. **Learn the tells** — Memorize the taxonomy (collapse to one variable, absurd absolute, mind-reading, word salad, laundry list, etc.) (ep 81)
2. **Identify the trigger** — What new information caused the dissonance? (ep 90)
3. **Track prediction records** — Whose filter predicted this correctly? (ep 65, 81, 89)
4. **Use the TDS escape route** — Walk through the hoax ladder: "If Charlottesville was wrong, what about Russiagate? If Russiagate was wrong..." (ep 69)
5. **Apply the "and" test** — Does the complaint have a second part explaining the actual harm? (ep 107)
6. **Apply the halfpinion test** — Does the critic offer an alternative? (ep 107)
7. **Check for the laundry list** — If they need 5+ complaints, none of them are strong enough individually. (ep 106)

### Sequence 3: The Diplomatic Persuasion Model (North Korea)

Taught across episodes 75-108, this is Adams's real-time analysis of persuasion applied to geopolitics:

1. **Establish wanting → deciding** — Shift from "we'd like peace" to "we've decided there will be peace" (ep 73, 75, 80)
2. **Build trust through reversible giveaways** — Both sides trade air to test reliability (ep 102)
3. **Remove the reason** — Eliminate why the opponent needs the thing you want them to give up (ep 108)
4. **Flattery loop** — Each side praises the other, talking themselves into the relationship (ep 65, 103)
5. **Ego as tool** — Ramp down ego for diplomacy; praise the opponent publicly (ep 80, 82, 101)
6. **Design for audience of one** — Tailor every communication to the actual decision-maker, not observers (ep 103, 104)
7. **Expand variables** — Add economic development, tourism, prestige to create more trade space (ep 41, 43, 55, 75, 80, 98)
8. **Think past the sale** — The Singapore video showed life after peace, not the negotiation process (ep 101, 103)
9. **Strategic niceness** — Once winning, lead with warmth (ep 90, 91, 92)

### Sequence 4: Identity and Persuasion

Adams's most sociologically ambitious framework, taught mainly in episodes 44 and 104:

1. **Two identities** — Personal identity + group/tribal identity (ep 104)
2. **Group identity constrains strategy** — If your tribal story involves exploitation, certain strategies (like reciprocity) feel psychologically impossible (ep 104)
3. **Locked-in votes have no power** — When a group's political allegiance is assumed, they lose leverage (ep 44)
4. **Mental prisons maintain the lock** — "You can't talk to those people" keeps the prison intact (ep 44)
5. **Ego prevents escape** — Using ego as identity rather than tool makes it impossible to try new strategies (ep 104, 106)
6. **Respect frame enables action** — Reframe from identity-based ("racism") to behavior-based ("respect") (ep 92)

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: The Singapore Summit Video
**Episodes:** 101, 103, 104
**Techniques identified:** Visual persuasion, thinking past the sale, contrast principle, ego as tool, audience of one, "what if" framing, designed for North Korean aesthetic sensibility
**Adams's assessment:** "Perhaps one of the best diplomatic plays of all time"
**Key insight:** Critics evaluated the video as if it were made for American media consumption. It was made for Kim Jong-un. "Who else told you what I just told you? Who else said, 'Of course it looks weird to us, it's not for us'?"

### Case Study 2: "Punchy De Niro" Kill Shot
**Episodes:** 102, 103
**Techniques identified:** Kill shot naming, visual anchoring, behavioral confirmation, humor (2-of-6 rule), contrast (De Niro becoming what critics feared Trump would be)
**Adams's assessment:** "The stickiest nickname you've ever heard in your life"
**Key insight:** The nickname raised a genuinely concerning question (brain injury from boxing scenes → anger management) that made people research the connection, deepening the brand.

### Case Study 3: Children in Cages / Border Separation
**Episodes:** 105, 106, 107
**Techniques identified:** Visual persuasion (cage imagery), fear appeal, halfpinion (no alternative offered), fraudulent but effective persuasion, children vs. adult frames, Hayden's Auschwitz visual
**Adams's assessment:** "Really, really good" persuasion that is "completely fraudulent and lacking in factual accuracy... is still really effective. Because facts don't matter to persuasion."
**Key insight:** The proper counter is not fact-checking but pacing ("we agree, that's terrible") then leading ("so what should we do instead?"), which produces silence — "victory is sometimes silent."

### Case Study 4: Charlottesville "Fine People" Hoax
**Episodes:** 34, 36, 52, 69, 80
**Techniques identified:** Two movies on one screen, selective editing, pattern-based thinking vs. evidence-based thinking, hoax funnel
**Adams's assessment:** The media's interpretation of Trump's "fine people on both sides" comment omits that he explicitly excluded white supremacists in the same press conference. The hoax persists because it "fits a pattern" people already believe.

### Case Study 5: Spygate Branding
**Episodes:** 77
**Techniques identified:** Kill shot naming, forcing enemy to use your word, pre-suasion
**Adams's assessment:** By naming it "Spygate," Trump forced media to use his frame every time they discussed the story. Even debunking it requires saying the word.

### Case Study 6: Kanye West Breaking Mental Prisons
**Episodes:** 44
**Techniques identified:** Mental prison breaking, locked-in vote analysis, building trust across party lines
**Adams's assessment:** "Kanye is unlocking the way we think. That's what he's trying to do." The real impact wasn't celebrity endorsement but demonstrating that cross-party dialogue was permissible.

### Case Study 7: George Lakoff's Dilemma
**Episodes:** 106
**Techniques identified:** Expert trapped by own framework, facts-don't-matter paradox
**Adams's assessment:** Lakoff is "at a far higher level of understanding" than other anti-Trumpers because he recognizes Trump's skill. But "he still thinks facts matter" and therefore "can't sell that to his own side" who believe Trump is merely lucky.

### Case Study 8: The Trust-Building Phase of North Korea Negotiations
**Episodes:** 102, 103, 108
**Techniques identified:** Reversible giveaways, trust-first strategy, removing the reason, willing things into existence
**Adams's assessment:** Pundits scoring "he got two, this guy got three" are "very much missing the point of this first phase." The real game is building trust, not trading concessions. "Everything the president has done so far is to deal with the core problem. The core problem was always psychology."

### Case Study 9: Sam Harris and the Truth-Priority Trap
**Episodes:** 104
**Techniques identified:** Priorities framework (truth vs. winning), where facts work vs. don't, prediction of Harris's evolution
**Adams's assessment:** Harris's "prioritization of reason and truth are very strong principles that work in almost every realm of life except politics. In politics, it would be a disaster." Predicted Harris would gradually warm to Trump's results even while opposing his methods.

### Case Study 10: OIG Report as Two Movies Test Case
**Episodes:** 105
**Techniques identified:** Two movies on one screen, complexity enables competing narratives, Occam's Razor failure
**Adams's assessment:** "A 500-some-page report... that's all I needed to know. I would have said that means everybody's going to see whatever they want to see. In other words, when you have that many facts, they're all irrelevant."

## Frameworks Found

### 1. Systems vs Goals
**Core episodes:** 260, 286
**Description:** Adams's signature framework from *How to Fail at Almost Everything and Still Win Big*. Systems are repeatable processes that increase your odds over time; goals are specific outcomes that leave you in a constant state of pre-failure until achieved (and post-success letdown after).

- **Ep 260:** Explicit discussion referencing the book. "The President's Portfolio" is presented as a systems approach to governance -- instead of picking one policy and betting on it, you create a portfolio of initiatives so some will work even if others fail. "You want to test small and scale what works."
- **Ep 286:** Applied to the political divide itself. "The right is mostly about following a system that makes sense, whereas the left is more about the end state." Adams frames the left as goal-oriented (income equality, healthcare, education as specific targets) and the right as systems-oriented (capitalism, Constitution, courts as infrastructure that produces outcomes). "Experience will design a system that works for everybody as best you can in the long run. Someone young will just say, 'I want candy now.'"

**Key quote (Ep 286):** "Goals versus systems is the difference between youth and experience."

### 2. Talent Stack
**Core episodes:** 262, 264, 270
**Description:** Combining multiple mediocre skills into a rare, valuable combination. No single skill needs to be world-class; the stack itself creates differentiation.

- **Ep 262:** Dr. Shiva vs Elizabeth Warren comparison. Shiva has a talent stack of engineering, business, and political outsider credibility. Warren has academic credentials but a narrower stack. Adams analyzes how Shiva's diverse background creates a different kind of persuasive authority.
- **Ep 264:** Dr. Shiva interview -- his talent stack spans systems biology, engineering, entrepreneurship, and political ambition. Adams highlights how his MIT credentials in biological engineering give him unusual authority to discuss health/addiction topics.
- **Ep 270:** Trump's talent stack analyzed in detail: theater/performance + business deal-making + negotiation instinct + charisma + media manipulation. "No single one of these would be enough, but together they make him almost impossible to compete with."

### 3. Test Small
**Core episodes:** 260, 269, 271, 276
**Description:** Before committing to a large-scale policy or decision, run a small experiment. Reduce risk through iteration.

- **Ep 260:** Introduced as a principle from *How to Fail*. Applied to the President's Portfolio concept.
- **Ep 269:** Applied to drug policy -- test fentanyl/opioid solutions at small scale before national rollout. Applied to self-driving car proposals for Detroit ("test it in one city first").
- **Ep 271:** Applied to opioid legislation and postal loopholes for fentanyl from China.
- **Ep 276:** Applied to drug legalization. "Test it small. Pick a state, pick a city, see what happens."

**Key quote (Ep 260):** "You want to test small and scale what works."

### 4. Cultural Gravity
**Core episodes:** 259, 260
**Description:** The invisible pull of cultural norms that determines what's possible in a given society. Like physical gravity, you can fight it but it takes enormous energy. Better to align with it or redirect it.

- **Ep 259:** Introduced in context of Turkey, Saudi Arabia, and cannabis policy. Cultural gravity determines the pace of possible change.
- **Ep 260:** Applied to Kanye West's "ideation centers" idea -- cultural gravity determines whether a creative idea can take root.

### 5. President's Portfolio
**Core episodes:** 260, 265
**Description:** Governance as portfolio management rather than single-bet policy. Diversify initiatives so the overall portfolio succeeds even if individual bets fail.

- **Ep 260:** Primary exposition. Adams compares this to investment diversification and his own approach to projects.
- **Ep 265:** Referenced in healthcare context -- treat healthcare as a portfolio of approaches rather than one big plan.

### 6. Half-pinions
**Core episodes:** 265, 268
**Description:** Opinions formed without sufficient information. Adams's term for the premature opinions people form before the facts are in -- especially dangerous in politics.

- **Ep 265:** Concept introduced. People should hold "half-pinions" -- provisional, partial opinions -- instead of full commitments, especially on breaking news.
- **Ep 268:** Detailed definition and application. "A half-pinion is when you have an opinion but you don't have enough information to justify it being a full opinion."

### 7. 48-Hour Rule
**Core episodes:** 262, 276, 286
**Description:** After a controversial statement or event, wait 48 hours before forming a judgment. The initial facts are almost always incomplete or wrong.

- **Ep 262:** Applied to news cycles around political gaffes.
- **Ep 276:** Applied to the Megyn Kelly controversy. "If somebody says, 'Hey, that phrase was racially offensive,' then the person who said it should have 48 hours to say, 'I didn't even know it was racially offensive. I take it back.'"
- **Ep 286:** Applied to "cotton-pickin'" controversy. Adams uses the 48-Hour Rule framework explicitly.

### 8. Pleasure Unit Theory
**Core episode:** 264
**Description:** From the Dr. Shiva interview. A framework for understanding addiction and behavior through the lens of pleasure units -- people seek a baseline level of pleasure, and when natural sources are depleted (oxytocin, dopamine), they substitute artificial ones (drugs, food, screens).

- **Ep 264:** Shiva's framework ties together epigenetics, environmental cues, oxytocin deficiency, and addiction. Adams endorses it as a systems-level understanding of why people make bad health choices.

### 9. Home Detox Model
**Core episode:** 264
**Description:** From the Shiva interview. Instead of removing an addict from their environment for treatment (which fails because they return to the same triggers), redesign the home environment to remove cues that trigger addictive behavior.

- **Ep 264:** Presented as a systems approach to addiction -- change the environment rather than trying to change the person through willpower alone.

### 10. Laundry List Persuasion (10 x 0 = Banana)
**Core episode:** 284
**Description:** When someone presents a long list of accusations or evidence, each item individually weak, and claims the aggregate is overwhelming. Adams's counter: ten pieces of bad evidence don't add up to one piece of good evidence. "10 x 0 = Banana" -- multiplying zeros gives you nothing, no matter how many you have.

- **Ep 284:** Full exposition with the "One Best Evidence" challenge technique. When faced with a laundry list, ask: "What is your single best piece of evidence?" If the single best piece doesn't hold up, the list is worthless.

**Key quote (Ep 284):** "If you can't describe it succinctly, you don't understand it."

### 11. Click Filter
**Core episode:** 278
**Description:** The mechanism by which media coverage is shaped not by truth or importance but by what generates clicks. Creates a systematic distortion of reality.

- **Ep 278:** Adams frames media not as biased left or right but as biased toward engagement. The Click Filter explains why certain stories dominate -- not because they matter most but because they generate the most emotional reaction.

### 12. Edge of Doom = Edge of Victory
**Core episode:** 266
**Description:** Crises and apparent disasters often contain the seeds of opportunity. "Never waste a good crisis" extended into a reframing principle.

- **Ep 266:** "The edge of doom is the edge of victory" -- the moment things look worst is often the inflection point. Applied to geopolitics and personal strategy.

### 13. Cannibalizing Your Own Product
**Core episode:** 288
**Description:** Business strategy principle. If you have a successful product, you should be the one to replace it with something better -- because if you don't, a competitor will.

- **Ep 288:** Applied metaphorically to politics and voting rights. "In the business world, there's a phrase for that. If you make a product and it's hugely popular, the first thing you should think of doing is cannibalizing it."

### 14. Experienced Manager vs Rookie Manager
**Core episode:** 1220
**Description:** The rookie manager accepts expert timelines. The experienced manager says "do it faster" and then the expert does it faster. Applied to Trump's Operation Warp Speed.

- **Ep 1220:** Detailed exposition. FDA data review normally takes six months. Trump said no. "The inexperienced person's view of management: 'the person who knows more than I do says two months, I guess I accept that.' The experienced person's view: look blankly at the expert and say 'do it in one month.' And then the expert does it in one month."

**Key quote (Ep 1220):** "Just about everything can be done faster."

### 15. The 20% Rule (Persuadability)
**Core episode:** 1213, 1220
**Description:** Approximately 20% of any population can be persuaded of virtually anything by a skilled persuader. Not correlated with intelligence, education, or gullibility. No reliable way to identify the 20% in advance.

- **Ep 1213:** Applied to the simultaneous sip ritual. "Simply tell people what to feel and it'll work, just not on everybody." The key: it has to be something they would enjoy doing anyway.
- **Ep 1220:** Applied to Chinese espionage. "20% of all people -- politicians, smart people, dumb people -- can be convinced of anything. They can be convinced there's something in the room that isn't even there."

### 16. Diversification as Epistemology
**Core episode:** 1220
**Description:** Just as investment diversification protects against single-bet failure, information diversification protects against single-source distortion. You must consume news from left and right sources to have any idea what's happening.

- **Ep 1220:** "If you're not watching the news on the left and the right, you're not watching anything."

### 17. Alternating Leadership Styles
**Core episode:** 1220
**Description:** The optimal governance strategy may be alternating between different presidential styles (disruptive/deal-making vs steady/institutional), because each type can accomplish "sticky" things the other cannot.

- **Ep 1220:** "Your best situation is to alternate... they have different skills and there are some problems that some of them can solve the other cannot." Trump's sticky achievements (Middle East, Warp Speed, trade deals, judges) vs Biden's potential sticky achievements (nuclear energy, healthcare, tamping down unrest).

# Reframe Engine

Take any problem, conflict, or stuck situation and run it through Scott Adams' reframing toolkit. Adams's central claim: most problems are not hard to solve — they are hard to see correctly. Change the frame and the solution often becomes obvious.

## When to Use

The user is stuck. They have a problem they've been thinking about in one way and need a different angle. Or they want to understand a conflict where two sides seem irreconcilable. Or they want to stress-test their own framing before committing to a course of action.

## How to Run This

Ask: "Describe the problem or situation you're stuck on. What have you tried, and why isn't it working?"

Then run the situation through each reframing lens. Not every lens will apply — skip the ones that don't fit. Present the 2-3 most powerful reframes and let the user decide which one unlocks new action.

## The Reframing Toolkit

### Lens 1: The Persuasion Filter

**Reframe:** Stop analyzing the facts. Analyze the persuasion.

Adams's core insight is that most public conflicts are not about facts — they're about persuasion. People are not debating because one side has better evidence. They're debating because different persuasion techniques have captured different audiences.

**Apply when:** The user is frustrated that "the facts are on my side but nobody listens," or when two groups seem to be looking at the same evidence and reaching opposite conclusions.

**Questions:**
- Who benefits from this framing of the problem?
- What persuasion technique is keeping the current frame in place?
- If you replaced the factual argument with a visual, emotional, or identity-based frame, what would change?

### Lens 2: Two Movies on One Screen

**Reframe:** Both sides think the other side is crazy. What if they're watching different movies?

Adams's "two movies" concept: the same set of facts can produce completely opposite interpretations, and both feel absolutely certain to their holders. The problem isn't that one side is stupid — it's that different filters produce different movies.

**Apply when:** The user is in a conflict where both sides are intelligent, well-meaning people who cannot agree. Or when the user is baffled by how someone else could possibly think what they think.

**Questions:**
- What is your "movie" — the narrative that makes all the facts fit?
- What is the other person's "movie" — the narrative that makes all the SAME facts fit differently?
- Is there a third movie that accounts for both sets of observations?
- Adams's test: "Which movie predicts the future better?" Focus on predictive power, not who is more morally right.

### Lens 3: Systems vs. Goals

**Reframe:** Stop trying to achieve the goal. Design a system.

Adams's most widely cited life principle. Goals are for losers — a goal is a state of continuous failure until you succeed, and then it's over. A system is something you do every day that increases your odds of success over time, regardless of any specific outcome.

**Apply when:** The user is stuck in goal-oriented thinking ("I need to lose 30 pounds," "I need to close this deal," "I need to get promoted").

**Questions:**
- What's the goal you're trying to achieve?
- What would a daily SYSTEM look like that increases your odds of success over time, regardless of whether this specific goal works out?
- If this particular goal fails, does the system still leave you in a better position?
- Adams's test: "A good system is one where you come out ahead even if the specific goal fails."

### Lens 4: The Moist Robot Reframe

**Reframe:** Stop expecting people to be rational. Program them.

Adams's "moist robot hypothesis": humans are biological machines that respond predictably to inputs. We are not rational agents who weigh evidence and choose wisely. We are programmable systems that respond to visual imagery, emotion, repetition, social proof, and identity signals.

**Apply when:** The user is frustrated that someone "won't listen to reason" or "can't see the obvious truth." Or when they need to change someone's behavior.

**Questions:**
- What inputs are you currently providing this person?
- What outputs are you getting?
- If this person is a "moist robot" who responds to inputs mechanically, what different inputs would produce the desired output?
- Specifically: Have you tried changing the visual, the emotional frame, the identity appeal, or the social proof?

### Lens 5: The Talent Stack Reframe

**Reframe:** Stop competing on the dimension where you're losing. Create a new dimension.

**Apply when:** The user feels outclassed by competition, can't win on price/speed/quality, or is stuck in a head-to-head comparison they'll lose.

**Questions:**
- What dimension are you currently competing on?
- What adjacent dimensions could you add that your competitor can't easily match?
- What combination of "good enough" capabilities would create a unique offering?

### Lens 6: The Prediction Filter

**Reframe:** Stop arguing about who is right. Check whose model predicts better.

Adams's epistemological framework: we cannot know "base reality." We can only know which models predict the future better. The model with the better prediction track record deserves more weight, regardless of whether it "feels" right.

**Apply when:** The user is in a debate where both sides have compelling arguments and no way to resolve the disagreement through evidence.

**Questions:**
- What does your current model predict will happen next?
- What does the opposing model predict?
- When you check against past predictions, which model had a better track record?
- Adams's principle: "I can't tell you what's true. I can tell you what predicts."

### Lens 7: The Kill Shot Reframe

**Reframe:** Stop arguing the details. Find the one label that ends the debate.

Adams's "kill shot" is a single word or short phrase that permanently reframes a target. It works by being visual, containing a seed of truth, and being sticky enough to override all other framing.

**Apply when:** The user needs to simplify a complex position into something that wins instantly. Or when they need to counter a kill shot being used against them.

**Questions (for creating one):**
- What is the single most damaging true thing about the opposing position?
- Can you distill it to 2-3 words that create a visual?
- Does it have a "seed of truth" that the audience has already noticed but not articulated?

**Questions (for defending against one):**
- What kill shot label has been applied to you or your position?
- Is there a seed of truth in it? (If yes, address that first)
- Can you reframe with a counter-label, or is the high-ground maneuver a better response?

## Delivering the Reframes

After running through the applicable lenses, present the 2-3 most powerful reframes:

```
ORIGINAL FRAME: [How the user currently sees the problem]

REFRAME 1: [Lens Name]
[New way of seeing it, with specific next action]

REFRAME 2: [Lens Name]
[New way of seeing it, with specific next action]

REFRAME 3: [Lens Name]
[New way of seeing it, with specific next action]

RECOMMENDED: [Which reframe is most likely to unlock progress, and why]
```

Ask: "Which of these shifts how you're thinking about it? Or should we go deeper on one?"
